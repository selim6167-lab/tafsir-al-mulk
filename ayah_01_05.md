# Comparative Tafsir: Surah Al-Mulk (67), Ayahs 1-5

## Introduction

### Names of the Surah

The surah is known by several names across the tafsir literature. Its primary name is **سورة الملك** (Surah Al-Mulk), as it appears in most مصاحف and hadith collections (Ibn Ashur). It is also called **تبارك الذي بيده الملك**, after its opening words, as named by the Prophet (peace be upon him) in a hadith reported by al-Tirmidhi from Abu Hurayrah (Ibn Ashur, Zuhayli). A shortened form, **تبارك الملك**, combining the first and last significant words of the opening ayah, was also used in the Prophet's era (Ibn Ashur).

Ibn Ashur catalogues eight total names for this surah:

- **الملك** (the standard title)
- **تبارك الذي بيده الملك** (full opening sentence)
- **تبارك الملك** (abbreviated form)
- **المانعة** (the Protector), from Ibn Mas'ud's report: "We used to call it al-Mani'ah in the Prophet's time" (Tabrani via Ibn Ashur)
- **المنجية** (the Saviour), as the Prophet described it (Tirmidhi via Ibn Ashur, Zuhayli)
- **الواقية** (the Shield), mentioned alongside al-Munjiyah (Zamakhshari, Qurtubi, Zuhayli)
- **المنّاعة** (intensive form of al-Mani'ah), cited in Jamal al-Qurra' (Ibn Ashur)
- **المجادلة** (the Advocate), from Ibn Abbas, because it argues on behalf of its reciter in the grave (Zuhayli, Ibn Ashur via al-Razi)

### Virtues (فضائل)

Multiple tafasir cite the following ahadith on the surah's merits:

1. Abu Hurayrah narrates that the Prophet (peace be upon him) said: "A surah of the Quran, thirty ayahs, interceded for a man until he was forgiven: تبارك الذي بيده الملك" (Tirmidhi, graded حسن). Cited by Qurtubi, Zuhayli, Baghawi, Ibn Ashur.
2. A Companion pitched his tent over an unknown grave and heard someone inside reciting Surah al-Mulk to its end. The Prophet said: "هي المانعة هي المنجية تنجيه من عذاب القبر" (It is the Protector, the Saviour; it saves him from the punishment of the grave). Reported by Tirmidhi (حسن غريب). Cited by Qurtubi, Ibn Ashur, Zuhayli.
3. Ibn Abbas reports the Prophet said: "وددت أن تبارك الذي بيده الملك في قلب كل مؤمن" (I wish that Tabarak were in the heart of every believer). Reported by al-Tha'labi. Cited by Qurtubi.
4. Ibn Mas'ud said: When a deceased is placed in his grave, [the trial] approaches from his feet and his tongue says: "He used to recite Surah al-Mulk upon me." Then: "She is the protector from God's punishment." (Qurtubi)

### Makki/Madani Status and Connection to the Preceding Surah

The surah is Makki by consensus of all scholars (Qurtubi: "في قول الجميع"; Ibn Ashur cites Ibn Atiyyah and al-Qurtubi). A weak opinion via Juwaybar holds the entire surah is Madani, and an isolated report from al-Dahhak via Ibn Abbas states three ayahs were excepted, though they are not identified (Ibn Ashur).

Zuhayli identifies two connections to the preceding Surah al-Tahrim:

1. **General connection:** Both surahs demonstrate God's absolute power and sovereignty. Al-Tahrim shows God's support for His Messenger against two of his wives, while al-Mulk proclaims God's dominion over all creation.
2. **Specific connection:** Al-Tahrim closes with two exemplary pairs of women (believers and disbelievers), and al-Mulk opens by demonstrating God's encompassing knowledge and creative power, showing that He manifests whatever wonders He wills in His creation, regardless of the outward circumstances of individuals.

In the order of revelation, it is the 76th surah revealed, coming after Surah al-Mu'minun and before Surah al-Haqqah (Ibn Ashur).

## Ayah 1: تَبَارَكَ الَّذِي بِيَدِهِ الْمُلْكُ وَهُوَ عَلَى كُلِّ شَيْءٍ قَدِيرٌ

### Core Meaning

The surah opens with God glorifying Himself and declaring His absolute sovereignty. The verb تبارك (tafā'ala from بركة) conveys that God is transcendently exalted, abundant in goodness, and free from every deficiency (Maturidi, Safwat, Zamakhshari). Maturidi offers a nuanced etymology: بركة is a kinayah (metonymy) for the negation of every flaw, citing the Quran's description of rain as ماء مباركا (water free of impurity), so تبارك means God is exalted beyond having any likeness, partner, or imperfection.

The expression بيده الملك signifies God's total ownership and absolute disposal over all creation (Ibn Kathir, Qurtubi, Zuhayli). Ibn Abbas explains: "He dignifies whom He wills and humbles whom He wills, gives life and causes death, enriches and impoverishes, gives and withholds" (Qurtubi, Safwat). Zamakhshari reads اليد as مجاز (figurative) for complete encompassment and mastery over dominion, not a physical hand. Ibn Ashur notes that the word الملك covers both ملك (sovereign authority, the power to command and enforce) and مِلك (ownership), and God possesses both.

Maturidi devotes considerable space to refuting the Mu'tazili position that God does not grant sovereignty to disbelievers, arguing from multiple Quranic verses (تؤتي الملك من تشاء، أن آتاه الله الملك) that God's kingship is universal and unconstrained, and denying it would negate the very praise the ayah establishes.

The closing clause وهو على كل شيء قدير confirms God's omnipotence over all things, both existent and potential (Zamakhshari, Zuhayli). Maturidi again notes the Mu'tazili contradiction: by denying God's creative role in human actions, they effectively deny His universal power, making Him قادر على البعض (powerful over some things only), contradicting the absolute statement of the ayah.

### Linguistic Analysis

- **تبارك**: وزن تفاعل from the root ب-ر-ك. Al-Hasan al-Basri glossed it as تقدّس (Qurtubi). Others said it means دام, i.e., the Eternal with no beginning to His existence and no end to His permanence (Qurtubi). Zuhayli notes this word is exclusive to God and may never be applied to any creature. The وزن تفاعل here does not indicate reciprocity but rather intensification and self-attribution of the quality.
- **بيده الملك**: Zamakhshari identifies this as استعارة تمثيلية (representative metaphor), likening God's complete control to someone holding something in his hand. Zuhayli categorizes it similarly. Maturidi reads اليد as standing in place of المالك, i.e., "He who owns all sovereignty."
- **الذي**: The use of الموصول (relative pronoun) rather than a proper name achieves تفخيم وتعظيم (magnification), as noted by Zuhayli.
- **قدير**: صيغة المبالغة (intensive form) from the root ق-د-ر, indicating that His power is complete and inexhaustible.

### Points of Divergence

**Issue: Scope of الملك**

- View 1 (Majority): الملك here is universal, encompassing sovereignty over all creation in both the دنيا and the آخرة (Ibn Kathir, Qurtubi, Zuhayli, Safwat).
- View 2 (Muhammad ibn Ishaq, via Qurtubi): الملك here refers specifically to ملك النبوة (the dominion of prophethood), by which God honoured those who followed it and humiliated those who opposed it.

**Issue: Does God grant sovereignty to disbelievers?**

- View 1 (Ahl al-Sunnah / Maturidi): Yes. God's sovereignty is absolute. He grants worldly kingship to whomever He wills, believer or disbeliever. The pronoun in أن آتاه الله الملك refers to Nimrod, and the verse تؤتي الملك من تشاء is universal.
- View 2 (Mu'tazilah, as reported by Maturidi): God does not grant sovereignty to disbelievers. The pronoun in أن آتاه الله الملك refers to Ibrahim, not Nimrod. Maturidi refutes this at length.

### Unique Insights

- Maturidi distinguishes between ملك (sovereignty, authority to command) and مِلك (ownership, exclusive right of disposition), noting that a person may hold one without the other, but God holds both perfectly.
- Zuhayli observes that تبارك carries the meaning that God is a source of ever-increasing خير (good), from the root meaning of بركة (growth and increase), both material and spiritual.

## Ayah 2: الَّذِي خَلَقَ الْمَوْتَ وَالْحَيَاةَ لِيَبْلُوَكُمْ أَيُّكُمْ أَحْسَنُ عَمَلًا وَهُوَ الْعَزِيزُ الْغَفُورُ

### Core Meaning

God created death and life as the framework within which human beings are tested (Ibn Kathir, Qurtubi, Zuhayli, Safwat). The scholars agree that ليبلوكم (to test you) does not imply that God needs to acquire knowledge. Rather, it is a metaphor (استعارة): God's treatment of His creation resembles the treatment of an examiner, making manifest to the creatures themselves who among them is best in deed (Qurtubi, Maturidi, Zamakhshari, Ibn Ashur).

The meaning of أحسن عملا is significant. The Prophet (peace be upon him), upon reciting this verse, said: "أيكم أحسن عقلا وأورع عن محارم الله وأسرع في طاعة الله" (which of you has the best understanding, the most restraint from God's prohibitions, and the swiftest obedience to God) (Baghawi, Qurtubi, Zuhayli). Al-Fudayl ibn Iyad defined أحسن عملا as أخلصه وأصوبه (most sincere and most correct): sincere, meaning for God's sake alone, and correct, meaning according to the Sunnah. Neither quality alone suffices (Zamakhshari, Baghawi).

Maturidi offers a distinctive theological reading: God created death as an object of رهبة (dread) and life as an object of رغبة (desire). The test lies in both: whether one is deterred by the dread of death from evil, and drawn by the love of life toward the eternal life of the hereafter. Thus both death and life serve the purpose of ابتلاء.

The closing العزيز الغفور pairs might with mercy. العزيز is the Mighty who punishes those who persist in disobedience, while الغفور is the One who forgives those who repent (Ibn Kathir, Qurtubi, Baghawi). Ibn Ashur adds that this pairing is a تذييل (capping statement): العزيز addresses the portion of the test relevant to the disobedient (warning of retribution), while الغفور addresses the portion relevant to the obedient (promise of pardon). Maturidi notes that these names clarify that God's testing is not for His own benefit or honour, since He is العزيز in Himself, but for the benefit of the tested.

### Linguistic Analysis

- **خلق الموت والحياة**: Ibn Kathir and Zuhayli observe that the verb خلق used with الموت indicates that death is an existential reality (أمر وجودي), not mere non-existence. The majority of Ahl al-Sunnah hold death is a created entity that is the antithesis of life. Zamakhshari (representing the Mu'tazili view) defined death as عدم الحياة (the absence of life), a position that the marginal commentator (Ahmad ibn al-Munir, called الانتصاف) strongly refutes.
- **تقديم الموت على الحياة** (placing death before life): Multiple explanations are given. (1) Death is closer to القهر (subjugation), thus more fitting in a context of divine majesty (Qurtubi, Baghawi). (2) Death is temporally prior, since things begin in a state of non-existence, like the نطفة and the تراب (Qurtubi, Baghawi). (3) Death is a stronger motivator for good deeds, since contemplating one's mortality is the most powerful spur to action (Zamakhshari, Qurtubi, Zuhayli).
- **ليبلوكم أيكم أحسن عملا**: Zamakhshari analyses the grammatical relationship at length. The verb بلا (test) here subsumes the meaning of علم (know), and the clause أيكم أحسن عملا fills the role of the second مفعول به. He debates whether this constitutes تعليق (suspension) of the verb from its second object due to the intervening interrogative أيّ. Al-Farra and al-Zajjaj say أيّ is مرفوع بالابتداء (nominative as mubtada') and أحسن is its خبر, with an implied verb like فينظر (then He sees) between ليبلوكم and أيكم (Qurtubi, Baghawi, Ibn Ashur).

### Points of Divergence

**Issue: What does الموت والحياة refer to?**

- View 1 (Qatadah, via Ibn Kathir and Qurtubi): Death in the دنيا and life in the آخرة.
- View 2 (Abu Bakr al-Asam, via Maturidi): خلق الموت means "He created you as dead matter" (نطفة، علقة، مضغة), then granted you life.
- View 3 (Majority, via Safwat, Zuhayli): Death and life in the worldly sense: the cessation of the soul's attachment to the body, and its attachment to the body.
- View 4 (Maturidi's own): Both death and life are created for the purpose of ابتلاء. Death as a مرهب (deterrent) and life as a مرغب (motivator), each serving the test differently.
- View 5 (al-Zajjaj, via Qurtubi): The لام in ليبلوكم attaches only to الحياة. God created death for البعث والجزاء (resurrection and recompense) and life for الابتلاء (testing).

**Issue: Is death a positive entity or mere absence?**

- View 1 (Ahl al-Sunnah / Majority): Death is أمر وجودي (a positive, created entity), the opposite of life. Evidence: it is described with the verb خلق, and in the آخرة it will be brought in the form of a كبش أملح (ram) and slaughtered (Ibn Kathir, Zuhayli, Qurtubi).
- View 2 (Mu'tazilah / Zamakhshari): Death is عدم الحياة (absence of life). "Creating death" means removing the quality that makes something alive.

### Unique Insights

- Qurtubi cites a unique interpretation from al-Suddi: أحسن عملا means "most frequent in remembering death and best prepared for it, and most fearful and cautious of it."
- Qurtubi also reports a distinctive interpretation of the ابتلاء: God tests the servant through the death of those dear to him to reveal his صبر (patience), and through life to reveal his شكر (gratitude).
- Maturidi draws a remarkable theological chain: the creation of death and life proves the necessity of البعث (resurrection), because the aspirant can only reach what he aspires to through resurrection, and the one warned can only face what he is warned of through resurrection. Furthermore, it proves the necessity of الرسالة (prophethood), because the realities of reward and punishment are unseen and require a messenger to convey them.
- Ibn Ashur notes that the تعليل (purposive clause) ليبلوكم does not restrict God's purposes to this single reason, for a single divine act may have multiple حكم (wisdoms), and the one mentioned is selected for its relevance to the مقام (rhetorical context).

## Ayah 3: الَّذِي خَلَقَ سَبْعَ سَمَاوَاتٍ طِبَاقًا مَا تَرَى فِي خَلْقِ الرَّحْمَنِ مِنْ تَفَاوُتٍ فَارْجِعِ الْبَصَرَ هَلْ تَرَى مِنْ فُطُورٍ

### Core Meaning

This ayah shifts from the creation of human existence to the creation of the greatest visible phenomena: the seven heavens. They are described as طباقا, meaning layered one above the other (Ibn Kathir, Qurtubi, Baghawi) or matching and corresponding to one another in their orderly design (Ibn Ashur). Ibn Kathir raises the question of whether the heavens are متواصلات (contiguous) or متفاصلات (separated by space), and judges the latter view as sounder, citing the hadith of the اسراء.

The clause ما ترى في خلق الرحمن من تفاوت declares that there is no inconsistency, no discordance, and no defect in God's creation (Ibn Kathir, Safwat, Qurtubi). Zamakhshari explains that the original phrasing would be ما ترى فيهنّ (in them), but the Quran substituted في خلق الرحمن to achieve two things: (1) تعظيم خلقهن (to magnify the act of creating them), and (2) to indicate the reason for their freedom from defect, namely that they are the creation of الرحمن, whose overwhelming power produces only perfection.

Ibn Ashur extends this further: the use of the name الرحمن (rather than الله or a pronoun) is an إيماء (allusion) that the perfection of creation is itself an expression of God's رحمة (mercy), because if there were تفاوت (inconsistency) in creation, it would disrupt the نظام (system) upon which human welfare depends.

The command فارجع البصر هل ترى من فطور is a challenge to the observer: look again at the sky, can you find any شقوق (cracks) or صدوع (fractures)? (Ibn Kathir, Qurtubi, Baghawi). The الفاء is تفريعية (consequential), building on the negation that precedes it: since there is no تفاوت, go ahead and verify it yourself through observation (Zamakhshari, Ibn Ashur).

### Linguistic Analysis

- **طباقا**: Either a مصدر (infinitive) used as a وصف (descriptive), meaning "in a state of mutual correspondence" (Qurtubi, Zamakhshari), or a جمع of طبق like جمل/جمال (Qurtubi, Baghawi). Sibawayh read it as a second مفعول به for خلق taken in the sense of جعل (Qurtubi). Ibn Ashur proposes it could also be from طابق (to match), signifying that they are متماثلة (similar) in properties such as sphericity and orderly motion.
- **تفاوت / تفوّت**: Two قراءات. The majority read مِنْ تَفَاوُتٍ (tafāwut). Hamzah and al-Kisa'i read مِنْ تَفَوُّتٍ (tafawwut) without alif. Both are valid لغتان, like تعاهد/تعهد and تحامل/تحمل (Qurtubi, Baghawi). Abu Ubayd preferred the latter, citing the hadith of Abd al-Rahman ibn Abi Bakr, but al-Nahhas rejected his reasoning (Qurtubi). Ibn Ashur notes that the root meaning is الفوت (distance, separation), and the تفاعل form here indicates intensity, not reciprocity. The word is مستعار (borrowed) for the meaning of inconsistency and lack of proportion.
- **فطور**: Plural of فطر (a crack or split), from the root ف-ط-ر meaning to cleave open. Ibn Abbas: من وهي (weakness). Mujahid and al-Dahhak: شقوق (cracks). Qatadah: خلل (gaps). Al-Suddi: خروق (holes) (Ibn Kathir, Qurtubi). Qurtubi cites poetry: بنى لكم بلا عمد سماء / وزيّنها فما فيها فطور.
- **فارجع البصر**: The فاء here is تسبيبية (causative), linking the command to look back to the preceding negation of تفاوت (Zamakhshari, Ibn Ashur). The verb ارجع is transitive (رجع المتعدي), meaning "send back, redirect" (Ibn Ashur).

### Points of Divergence

**Issue: Are the seven heavens contiguous or separated?**

- View 1 (Ibn Kathir, preferred): They are متفاصلات (separated by space), as indicated by the hadith of the اسراء.
- View 2 (cited by Qurtubi from Ibn Abbas): They are ملتزق (joined at their edges).
- View 3 (Ibn Ashur): طباقا may refer not to physical stacking but to the systematic correspondence of celestial bodies in the solar system, which are at varying distances yet display matching properties (sphericity, orbital motion).

**Issue: Scope of ما ترى في خلق الرحمن من تفاوت**

- View 1 (Many mufassirun): The negation applies specifically to the heavens (Qurtubi reports this reading).
- View 2 (Zamakhshari, Ibn Ashur): The substitution of في خلق الرحمن for فيهنّ makes the clause a تذييل (universal conclusion): all of God's creation, not only the heavens, is free from inconsistency.
- View 3 (Maturidi): There is no تفاوت in the sense of دلالة (indicative function): all created things point uniformly to God's وحدانية and حكمة, even though they differ in their physical attributes (the heavens differ from the earth, life differs from death). The unity lies in the coherence of purpose and design.

### Unique Insights

- Maturidi builds a remarkable logical argument: if one observed فطور (cracks) in creation, one would suspect multiple creators (تمانع / competing deities), because conflict and fracture arise from plurality. The absence of فطور is thus a direct proof of توحيد.
- Maturidi also distinguishes between بصر الوجه (physical sight) and بصر القلب (insight of the heart), suggesting the command ارجع البصر may refer primarily to intellectual reflection. The observer has already seen the sky with his eyes; now he is being asked to reflect with his heart on what that sight implies for God's unity and wisdom.
- Ibn Ashur notes that the use of هل in هل ترى من فطور intensifies the interrogation, since هل carries the force of قد in questions, pushing the addressee beyond a casual glance to sustained investigation.

## Ayah 4: ثُمَّ ارْجِعِ الْبَصَرَ كَرَّتَيْنِ يَنْقَلِبْ إِلَيْكَ الْبَصَرُ خَاسِئًا وَهُوَ حَسِيرٌ

### Core Meaning

After the initial command to look, God commands the observer to repeat the examination again and again. The result is always the same: the gaze returns to the observer defeated and exhausted, having found nothing amiss (Ibn Kathir, Safwat, Zuhayli).

Ibn Abbas says خاسئا means ذليلا (humbled). Mujahid and Qatadah say صاغرا (disgraced) (Ibn Kathir). The word is derived from خسأ, to drive away (as one drives away a dog), thus conveying that the searching eye is rebuffed and repelled from finding any flaw (Qurtubi). حسير means كليل (worn out, dim), from الحسور (exhaustion). Ibn Abbas, Mujahid, Qatadah, and al-Suddi all agree it means المنقطع من الإعياء (spent from fatigue) (Ibn Kathir, Baghawi, Qurtubi).

Zamakhshari eloquently captures the image: the gaze is not merely unable to find defects; it is actively repelled, "as if driven away with صغار and قماءة (abasement and lowliness)," and simultaneously exhausted from prolonged searching. The twin qualities of خاسئ and حسير combine repulsion (failure to find) and fatigue (from the effort of searching).

### Linguistic Analysis

- **كرتين**: Formally a تثنية (dual) of كرّة (a return, a charge, from الكرّ), but used here for التكثير (multiplicity), not for the literal number two (Zamakhshari, Qurtubi, Zuhayli, Ibn Ashur). Evidence: the outcome described (خاسئا وهو حسير) implies exhaustion from many repetitions, not merely two glances. The analogy is to لبيك وسعديك, which does not mean "twice" but "again and again" (Zamakhshari, Ibn Ashur). Zuhayli notes that كرتين is منصوب في موضع المصدر (in the position of a مصدر), i.e., equivalent to "return the gaze with repeated returns."
- **ينقلب**: A مجزوم (jussive) verb, the جواب الطلب (apodosis of the command): "if you return your gaze repeatedly, it will come back..." (Zuhayli). Ibn Ashur explains that ينقلب was chosen over يرجع to avoid repetition of the root رجع already used twice in the passage, a feature he attributes to خصائص الإعجاز (qualities of Quranic inimitability). Similarly, كرتين was chosen over مرتين because كرة is less commonly associated with the literal number two, making it a clearer vehicle for the meaning of التكثير (Ibn Ashur).
- **خاسئا**: حال (circumstantial accusative) from خسأ. It can be both لازم and متعدّ (intransitive and transitive): خسأت الكلب (I drove the dog away) and خسأ الكلب (the dog slunk away) (Qurtubi). Zamakhshari links it to الصغار والقماءة (humiliation and contemptibility).
- **حسير**: صيغة فعيل, which can function as either فاعل (حاسر, one who is exhausted) or مفعول (محسور, one whom distance has worn out) (Qurtubi).

### Points of Divergence

**Issue: Is كرتين literal (twice) or figurative (many times)?**

- View 1 (Majority, including Zamakhshari, Qurtubi, Ibn Ashur, Zuhayli): It is figurative, meaning "repeatedly, again and again." The dual is used as كناية عن التكثير.
- View 2 (Maturidi): It could be literal, referring to two distinct types of looking: one by day and one by night, since the signs visible at each time differ. Or: one look with the physical eye and a second with the eye of the heart.
- View 3 (al-Hasan, al-Asam, via Maturidi): كرتين means "مرة بعد مرة" perpetually, i.e., a command to be in a constant state of contemplation and reflection on God's creation.

**Issue: Is the "looking" physical or intellectual?**

- View 1 (Most mufassirun): Physical sight directed at the sky, accompanied by reflection (Zuhayli, Ibn Kathir, Safwat).
- View 2 (Maturidi, preferred): The more fitting reading is بصر القلب (insight of the heart), because the observer has already physically seen the sky and knows it has no cracks. The command now pushes him to reflect intellectually on what the absence of فطور implies about God's unity and wisdom.
- View 3 (Maturidi, alternative): The first command (فارجع البصر) is physical sight; the second (ارجع البصر كرتين) is intellectual sight. The physical observation triggers the intellectual reflection.

### Unique Insights

- Zamakhshari uses the analogy of a military charge (كرّ) to explain the word كرّة: the gaze "charges" at the sky like a warrior pressing the attack, then retreats defeated, finding no weakness in the enemy's defences.
- Ibn Ashur notes two instances of lexical precision unique to this passage: (1) كرتين was chosen over مرتين because it resists the literal dual reading, and (2) ينقلب was chosen over يرجع to avoid repetition. Both choices demonstrate بلاغة إعجازية (rhetorical inimitability).
- Qurtubi cites a line of poetry that perfectly captures the meaning: مَنْ مَدَّ طَرْفًا إِلَى مَا فَوْقَ غَايَتِهِ / ارْتَدَّ خَسْآنَ مِنْهُ الطَّرْفُ قَدْ حَسِرَا ("Whoever stretches his gaze beyond his limit, his gaze returns to him repelled and worn").

## Ayah 5: وَلَقَدْ زَيَّنَّا السَّمَاءَ الدُّنْيَا بِمَصَابِيحَ وَجَعَلْنَاهَا رُجُومًا لِلشَّيَاطِينِ وَأَعْتَدْنَا لَهُمْ عَذَابَ السَّعِيرِ

### Core Meaning

After establishing that the heavens are flawless, God now describes what adorns them and the functions He assigned to the stars. The discussion moves from the perfection of structure to the beauty and purpose of contents (Ibn Ashur). The scholars identify three functions of the stars, based on a widely cited narration from Qatadah: (1) زينة للسماء (adornment for the sky), (2) رجوما للشياطين (missiles against devils), and (3) علامات يهتدى بها (signs for navigation). Qatadah added: "Whoever interprets them beyond these has burdened himself with what he has no knowledge of" (Ibn Kathir, Safwat, Qurtubi, Baghawi, Zuhayli).

The heavens are called السماء الدنيا (the nearest heaven) because it is the one closest to the people of earth, not because its counterpart is "the heaven of the آخرة" (Maturidi clarifies this explicitly, noting that the opposite of الدنيا in this context is القصوى, not الآخرة).

The verb زيّنا (We adorned) conveys both praise for the Creator's artistry and a reminder of His grace upon humanity. Ibn Ashur describes this as إدماج (interweaving) of الامتنان (gratitude-inducement) within the الاستدلال (argumentation).

Regarding رجوما للشياطين, a critical question arises: if the stars are permanent fixtures, how can they also be projectiles? The scholars offer several answers:

1. The shooting stars (شهب) that strike the devils are not the actual stars (كواكب) but emanations from them, comparable to a spark taken from a fire that remains intact (Ibn Kathir, Zamakhshari, Safwat, Qurtubi).
2. The pronoun ها in وجعلناها refers to the جنس (genus) of مصابيح, not to the identical individual stars that are the fixed adornments (Ibn Kathir).
3. Abu Ali al-Farisi said the shooting occurs from the stars themselves, but without diminishing their light or form (Qurtubi).

The ayah closes with وأعتدنا لهم عذاب السعير, meaning that in addition to being pelted with شهب in this world, the devils face the prepared punishment of the blazing fire in the آخرة (all tafasir). Maturidi notes the logical structure: even the punishment of being struck by شهب in this world does not discharge the full penalty. The fire of the hereafter remains as a separate, permanent retribution.

### Linguistic Analysis

- **السماء الدنيا**: الدنيا is the feminine of الأدنى (nearest), not derived from الدنيا (worldly life). It means the sky nearest to earth (Zamakhshari, Baghawi, Zuhayli, Maturidi, Ibn Ashur).
- **مصابيح**: Plural of مصباح (lamp/lantern), a تشبيه بليغ (compressed simile) comparing stars to lamps because of their luminosity (Ibn Ashur). The تنكير (indefiniteness) of مصابيح conveys تعظيم (grandeur): "What lamps! Lamps whose brilliance no earthly lamp can match" (Zamakhshari).
- **رجوما**: Plural of رجم, a مصدر used as an اسم (i.e., ما يُرجم به, "that which is used for pelting"), similar to خلق meaning مخلوق (Ibn Ashur, Qurtubi). Zuhayli notes it functions as a مفعول لأجله or حال.
- **أعتدنا**: From the root ع-ت-د, meaning "to prepare, make ready." The past tense indicates that the punishment is already prepared and waiting (Safwat, Baghawi).
- **السعير**: From the root س-ع-ر, meaning to kindle or set ablaze. It is both an adjective (مسعورة) and a name for one of the levels of hellfire (Qurtubi).
- **Rhetorical devices** (Zuhayli): The entire passage ayahs 3-5 exhibits إطناب (amplification) through the repetition of ارجع البصر. The endings قدير / حسير / السعير and الغفور / فطور constitute سجع مرصّع (ornamental rhyme). There is also طباق (antithesis) between الموت and الحياة.

### Points of Divergence

**Issue: Does the pronoun in وجعلناها refer to the actual stars or to something else?**

- View 1 (Ibn Kathir): The pronoun refers to the جنس (genus) of مصابيح generally, not to the specific individual stars that adorn the sky. The شهب are مستمدة (derived) from the stars but are not the stars themselves.
- View 2 (Abu Ali al-Farisi, via Qurtubi): The pronoun does refer to the stars themselves, but what detaches from them to strike the devils does not diminish their light or form.
- View 3 (al-Qushayri, via Qurtubi): They function as adornment before being used as projectiles, suggesting a temporal distinction.
- View 4 (Zamakhshari): The شهب are flames that separate from the stars the way a firebrand is taken from a fire, and the fire remains whole. This is confirmed by the Quranic verse: إلا من خطف الخطفة فأتبعه شهاب ثاقب.

**Issue: Is there a second meaning of رجوما للشياطين?**

- View 1 (Majority): Literal, physical pelting of الشياطين (jinn who attempt to eavesdrop on heavenly councils).
- View 2 (Zamakhshari, alternative): Some interpreted it figuratively as رجوما بالغيب (guessing in the dark), meaning the stars are made into tools of ظنون (conjectures) for شياطين الإنس (human devils, i.e., astrologers who claim to read the future in the stars).

### Unique Insights

- Maturidi identifies three distinct نعم (blessings) embedded in the stars: (1) beauty for the beholders, especially at night when the beauty of the earth is hidden; (2) guidance in the darkness of land and sea; (3) protection from the devils' corruption of heavenly news, ensuring that revelation reaches humanity pure and uncontaminated through the prophets rather than corrupted through the كهّان (soothsayers).
- Maturidi makes an additional subtle point: the beauty of the sky at night is a divine compensation. When the زينة of the earth (its colours and forms) becomes invisible in darkness, God replaces it with a different, even more remarkable beauty in the sky, one that is visible from afar, unlike earthly beauty that requires proximity.
- Maturidi also draws a parallel between the ابتلاء of the people of the earth and the people of the heavens: just as the earth and its adornments are a test for its inhabitants (إنا جعلنا ما على الأرض زينة لها لنبلوهم أيهم أحسن عملا), so too the زينة of the heavens tests the inhabitants of the heavens.
- Ibn Ashur observes that the تأكيد (emphasis) introduced by لقد at the beginning of this ayah is rhetorically connected to the preceding هل (which carries the force of قد in questions). The answer to "Do you see any فطور?" is emphatically confirmed: "Indeed, We have adorned it." The passage moves from negating defect to affirming beauty.
- Zamakhshari's image of the sky as "the roof of the house in which you gather" (سقف الدار التي اجتمعتم فيها) adorned with lamps of incomparable brightness, adds a visceral, domestic concreteness to the cosmic scene.
