# Surah Al-Mulk (67): Ayahs 6-11

## The Failed Test

Ayah 2 established the surah's governing idea: ليبلوكم أيكم أحسن عملا. Everything that follows is structured around that test. Ayahs 3-5 described the cosmos as evidence available to the tested. Now, ayahs 6-11 turn to the outcome for those who failed. The passage moves through a tightly constructed sequence: sentence (6), sensory horror (7-8a), interrogation (8b), confession (9-10), verdict (11). At its centre stands the most devastating line in the passage, where the damned admit they never used the very faculties God gave them for the test.

---

## Ayah 6

> وَلِلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا بِرَبِّهِمْ عَذَابُ جَهَنَّمَ وَبِئْسَ الْمَصِيرُ

The previous ayah ended with وأعتدنا لهم عذاب السعير, threatening the شياطين pelted by shooting stars. This ayah widens the lens. Ibn Ashur identifies it as a تتميم (rhetorical completion): lest anyone imagine the punishment belongs to devils alone, the Quran extends it to every كافر, from الإنس and الجن alike (Zamakhshari, Zuhayli). The shift from عذاب السعير to عذاب جهنم is deliberate. It signals a new, broader audience for the threat (Ibn Ashur).

The fronting of the prepositional phrase (وللذين كفروا) is not accidental. It serves الاهتمام والمبادرة: the urgency of identifying who the punishment belongs to before naming what it is (Ibn Ashur). You know who is condemned before you know their sentence.

Maturidi offers an unusual gloss on المصير. Where most read it as المآل والمنقلب (the final destination), he reads it as الطريق (the path). The wretchedness, on his reading, is not confined to the endpoint. It runs through the entire trajectory of disbelief.

## Ayah 7

> إِذَا أُلْقُوا فِيهَا سَمِعُوا لَهَا شَهِيقًا وَهِيَ تَفُورُ

The first detail of Hell is acoustic. Before the damned feel anything, they hear it. The verb ألقوا (they are cast) is passive, and Zamakhshari draws out the image: they are thrown in as حطب (firewood) is thrown into a great fire. They are not entering; they are being discarded.

What they hear is شهيق: technically, the raw intake of breath in the chest, as opposed to زفير, which occurs in the throat (Qurtubi). Applied to the fire, it produces an extraordinary image. Ibn Abbas, via Qurtubi, offers the most visceral comparison in the tradition: جهنم shrieks toward the disbelievers the way a mule brays at barley (تشهق إليهم شهقة البغلة للشعير), then exhales a زفرة so terrifying that no one remains unafraid. The simile is not arbitrary. A mule braying at barley does so out of craving. The fire has appetite.

Ibn Ashur notes a grammatical subtlety that deepens the horror. The لام in سمعوا لها indicates that the شهيق belongs to the fire itself (شبه ملك), not to its inhabitants. Maturidi, characteristically, declines to settle the question. A horrifying sound terrifies regardless of whether its source has consciousness or not; the distinction does not matter for the purpose of the ayah. His point is epistemological: we need not determine the mechanics of غيب realities when their effect is the same either way.

The second detail is kinetic: وهي تفور. It boils with them, al-Thawri explains, as grain boils in abundant water (Ibn Kathir). The people inside are the grain.

## Ayah 8

> تَكَادُ تَمَيَّزُ مِنَ الْغَيْظِ كُلَّمَا أُلْقِيَ فِيهَا فَوْجٌ سَأَلَهُمْ خَزَنَتُهَا أَلَمْ يَأْتِكُمْ نَذِيرٌ

The fire nearly tears itself apart from rage. The verb تميّز (أصله تتميّز) means that its parts nearly separate from one another (Ibn Kathir). Zamakhshari supplies the Arabic idiom behind it: يتميز غيظا ويتقصف غضبا, and the hyperbolic image of someone so furious that a piece of him flew into the earth and a piece into the sky. Ibn Ashur classifies the entire construction as تمثيلية مكنية (compound implied metaphor): the fire's state of seething, leaping, and consuming is mapped onto the image of a furious person who leaves nothing in reach unharmed. He draws a parallel to another Quranic personification: جدارا يريد أن ينقض (al-Kahf 77), where a wall "wants" to collapse.

The most theologically interesting question is whether the غيظ is figurative or real. The majority (Zamakhshari, Ibn Ashur, Zuhayli) read it as تمثيل, a figure of speech. Maturidi disagrees, or at least refuses to foreclose the literal possibility. God, he argues, can endow any creation with genuine awareness of His majesty. He made fire welcoming to Ibrahim; He can make it genuinely rage against His enemies. Then Maturidi draws a moral derivation unique to his tafsir: if even an inanimate creation rages on behalf of God, the believers have an even greater obligation to feel شدة for His sake. He cites أشداء على الكفار رحماء بينهم. The fire's غيظ is a mirror for the believer's own commitment.

Maturidi also identifies a second wisdom in the description. God portrays the شدة of the fire in this world precisely so that no one can claim on the Day of Judgement: إنا كنا عن هذا غافلين ("we were heedless of this"). The warning is itself part of the حجة.

The second half of the ayah shifts from the fire's character to a recurring scene. Every time a فوج (group) of disbelievers is cast in, the خزنة (Malik and his angelic wardens) ask them: ألم يأتكم نذير? The question is not for information. It is توبيخ وتقريع, rebuke piled on rebuke, adding حسرة to their torment (all sources). The scene repeats with every new group. The word كلما ensures this: it is not a single event but a pattern that continues indefinitely.

## Ayah 9

> قَالُوا بَلَى قَدْ جَاءَنَا نَذِيرٌ فَكَذَّبْنَا وَقُلْنَا مَا نَزَّلَ اللَّهُ مِنْ شَيْءٍ إِنْ أَنْتُمْ إِلَّا فِي ضَلَالٍ كَبِيرٍ

The answer comes instantly. بلى, yes, a warner did come. Ibn Ashur notices that the subsequent قد جاءنا نذير merely restates what بلى already conveys, and he identifies this repetition as تكرير الكلام عند التحسر: the stammering redundancy of remorse. They are not building an argument; they are reliving a regret.

What makes this confession devastating is its specificity. They do not simply admit they were warned. They reproduce the exact words of their rejection: ما نزّل الله من شيء. The من is زائدة for تأكيد النفي, intensifying the negation to its absolute form (Ibn Ashur). They denied not merely a particular revelation but the very possibility that God would reveal anything to any human, echoing the broader Quranic indictment: ما أنزل الله على بشر من شيء (al-An'am 91).

Ibn Kathir frames the entire exchange as proof of God's عدل (justice). He never punishes without first establishing the حجة through the sending of رسل, as confirmed by وما كنا معذبين حتى نبعث رسولا (al-Isra 15). The dialogue with the خزنة is not dramatic embellishment; it is the public establishment of a legal fact. The damned themselves testify that the proof was delivered and they refused it.

Maturidi adds a psychological dimension no other mufassir matches. In the afterlife, the كفار first tried lying under oath: والله ربنا ما كنا مشركين, hoping that false oaths might avail them as they had in the world. When that failed and they were cast into the fire, they switched to honest confession, hoping الاعتراف might spare them. Neither strategy works. The passage maps a trajectory of desperation: denial, then confession, then despair.

**On the closing إن أنتم إلا في ضلال كبير:** who says this? The question has genuine consequences for the scene. The majority (Zamakhshari primarily, Qurtubi, Zuhayli, Baghawi) read it as part of the disbelievers' recollection of what they used to say to the messengers in the world: "You are in nothing but great error." Ibn Ashur prefers a different reading. He argues that these are the words of the خزنة, resuming their speech after the فوج's panicked interruption. The evidence is grammatical: the re-introduction of وقالوا in ayah 10 would be unnecessary if the disbelievers were still speaking continuously from ayah 9. On his reading, the فوج blurted out their confession (بلى قد جاءنا نذير فكذبنا...), and the خزنة then deliver a verdict: "You were in nothing but great error." The scene becomes an interrogation with sentence, not a monologue of regret.

## Ayah 10

> وَقَالُوا لَوْ كُنَّا نَسْمَعُ أَوْ نَعْقِلُ مَا كُنَّا فِي أَصْحَابِ السَّعِيرِ

This is the confession that echoes through the entire surah. The damned admit they failed the ابتلاء of ayah 2 because they never used the tools they were given.

The significance runs deeper than the immediate context. In ayah 23, God will say: قل هو الذي أنشأكم وجعل لكم السمع والأبصار والأفئدة قليلا ما تشكرون. He gave them hearing, sight, and hearts. Here in ayah 10, the failed test-takers confess: لو كنا نسمع أو نعقل. They name the exact faculties that ayah 23 says God created for them. The structural echo is unmistakable. The test of ayah 2 was conducted through the equipment of ayah 23, and the failure is confessed in ayah 10. The surah folds back on itself: creation of the tools, the test, and the admission of not using them form a single arc.

But what does نسمع أو نعقل actually mean here? They obviously possessed ears and brains. Maturidi makes the crucial distinction. They had already admitted بلى قد جاءنا نذير, confirming that they heard and understood. The negation is not of the faculties themselves but of الانتفاع (benefiting from them). They heard but did not respond (لم يجيبوا لما سمعوا). They understood but did not act (لم يقوموا بوفاء ما عقلوا). The faculties were present; the will to use them was absent. Maturidi explicitly rejects the alternative reading that they mean "had we heard in the afterlife as we now hear," arguing that the آخرة is not a دار إسماع وإفهام (a realm of instruction). The confession is about the world they lived in, not the one they inhabit now.

Ibn Ashur unpacks the same point through a different lens. نسمع is not mere physical hearing but سمع خاص, the hearing of one who genuinely receives and considers. نعقل is not raw intelligence but عقل خاص, the reasoning of one who actually deliberates. Their إعراض (turning away) from the messengers' call functioned as if they did not hear at all, and their refusal to تدبّر functioned as if they had no عقل. He draws a broader conclusion: the polytheists were, objectively, the least rational people, because they rejected what could save them when their own religion offered no countervailing threat. There was nothing in شرك that punished you for leaving it; they had nothing to lose by listening, and they still refused.

Zamakhshari reads نسمع and نعقل as the two pillars of religious obligation: أدلة السمع (scriptural evidence, received through hearing) and أدلة العقل (rational evidence, grasped through intellect). The مدار التكليف (basis of obligation) rests on both. Then he turns sharp. He dismisses as بدع التفاسير (heretical interpretations) the claim that نسمع refers to أصحاب الحديث and نعقل to أصحاب الرأي, mocking the anachronism: "as if this ayah was revealed after these two schools appeared."

The sequence نسمع before نعقل is not arbitrary. Zuhayli explains: the one being called hears the caller's words first, then reflects upon them. Ibn Ashur adds that السمع is to العقل as the particular is to the universal, following the natural temporal order of reception before reflection.

## Ayah 11

> فَاعْتَرَفُوا بِذَنْبِهِمْ فَسُحْقًا لِأَصْحَابِ السَّعِيرِ

The scene closes with a verdict. The first word, فاعترفوا, arrives with a فاء that Ibn Ashur calls فصيحة (eloquent): it implies an entire elided clause. "Since they said all that, it has become clear that they confessed." The confession is not a new act; it is the Quran stepping back to name what the preceding dialogue already demonstrated.

The word ذنبهم is singular, but the meaning is plural. Zuhayli explains two reasons: when a مصدر is added to a plural (إضافة المصدر إلى الجمع), the collective attachment suffices for the plural meaning; and ذنب as a مصدر naturally encompasses both one and many. The specific ذنب is الكفر وتكذيب الأنبياء (Zuhayli, Qurtubi).

Then comes the sentence: فسحقا لأصحاب السعير. The root س-ح-ق carries the sense of extreme distance. All agree this is God's own pronouncement, not the words of the كفار or the خزنة. It functions as a دعاء (imprecation): "May God distance them from His mercy" (Ibn Kathir, Qurtubi, Zuhayli). Ibn Ashur suggests it may also serve as تعجيب (an expression of astonished contempt), in the way one says قاتله الله without literally invoking killing. He adds a third possibility: that it is spoken to them يوم الحساب after their اعتراف, as a تنديم (deepening of remorse) that adds psychological pain beyond the physical حريق. The fire burns their bodies; this word burns their dignity.

The second فاء is للتسبب (causal): because they confessed, therefore, away with them (Ibn Ashur). And critically, Zamakhshari notes that the confession itself changes nothing: سواء اعترفوا أو جحدوا فإن ذلك لا ينفعهم. Whether they admit or deny, the sentence stands. The اعتراف is real but futile, which is precisely what makes the scene so devastating. They finally see the truth, and it cannot save them.

Ibn Ashur observes that لأصحاب السعير functions as a تذييل (generalizing conclusion). The curse is not addressed only to the specific فوج in the dialogue but to all أصحاب السعير across all nations, giving the statement universal scope.

A minority tradition, transmitted through Sa'id ibn Jubayr and Abu Salih (via Qurtubi), holds that السحق is not abstract distance but a concrete واد في جهنم (a valley in Hell). If accepted, the imprecation becomes geographical: not "away with them" but "to that specific abyss with them."

Ibn Kathir closes his treatment of the passage with two prophetic traditions that capture its theological core. The first: "People will not perish until they have been given full excuse against themselves" (لن يهلك الناس حتى يعذروا من أنفسهم, Ahmad). The second: "No one enters the Fire except that he knows the Fire is more fitting for him than Paradise" (لا يدخل أحد النار إلا وهو يعلم أن النار أولى به من الجنة, Ahmad). The damned are not confused. They know exactly where they belong.

## Structural Note: The Test Cycle

Ayahs 6-11 complete the first arc of the surah's ابتلاء structure. Ayah 2 announced the test. Ayahs 3-5 presented the evidence (the flawless heavens, the adorned sky). Ayahs 6-11 show what happens to those who looked at all of that and walked away.

The confession in ayah 10 (لو كنا نسمع أو نعقل) is not merely a lament. It is the inverse of the test conditions. God gave السمع والأبصار والأفئدة (ayah 23) as instruments for passing the ابتلاء. The failed test-takers confess that they never deployed them. The surah will return to this point in ayah 23 with an accusation (قليلا ما تشكرون), but the confession has already been extracted here, from the mouths of the condemned themselves.
