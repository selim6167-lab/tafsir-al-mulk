# Comparative Tafsir: Surah Al-Mulk, Ayahs 25-30

**Sources:** Ibn Kathir, Qurtubi, Zuhayli, Zamakhshari, Baghawi, Ibn Ashur, Maturidi, Safwat al-Tafasir

---

## Ayah 25: وَيَقُولُونَ مَتَى هَذَا الْوَعْدُ إِنْ كُنْتُمْ صَادِقِينَ

### Core Meaning

After the preceding ayahs established God's power through creation, distribution of mankind across the earth, and the certainty of the حشر (gathering), the disbelievers respond with mockery and denial. They ask, "When will this promise come to pass, if you are truthful?" The مفسرون agree that this question was not a genuine inquiry but an expression of استهزاء (mockery) and استنكار (denial) (Zuhayli, Safwat, Maturidi). The الوعد (promise) refers either to the حشر (resurrection/gathering) implied by the preceding وَإِلَيْهِ تُحْشَرُونَ, or to the threatened عذاب (punishment) such as the خسف (swallowing by the earth) and the حاصب (stone-hurling wind) mentioned earlier (Zuhayli). Ibn Ashur notes that they persisted in this question repeatedly, which is why God reported it using the مضارع (present tense) form وَيَقُولُونَ, indicating تكرير (repetition and continuity). The خطاب (address) in إِنْ كُنْتُمْ صَادِقِينَ is directed at the Prophet (peace be upon him) and the believers, since they were the ones constantly warning of the يوم الحشر (Ibn Ashur).

Maturidi uniquely frames this verse as evidence of the Prophet's sincerity. He argues that their mockery, paradoxically, constitutes a دلالة نبوته (proof of his prophethood): had the Prophet been fabricating his message, he could have assigned the promise to a distant future date that would not expose him to refutation during his lifetime. Instead, he responded with إِنَّمَا الْعِلْمُ عِنْدَ اللَّهِ, showing that he only conveyed what was revealed to him and did not add to it from himself.

### Linguistic Analysis

- **مَتَى**: استفهام (interrogative), but used here in the sense of تهكم (sarcasm) and استنجاز (demanding fulfilment), not genuine inquiry (Ibn Ashur, Zuhayli).
- **الْوَعْدُ**: مصدر بمعنى اسم المفعول (masdar used in the sense of a passive participle), i.e. الموعود (that which is promised). It can refer to الحشر or العذاب (Ibn Ashur). Zuhayli specifies two possibilities: the حشر (resurrection), or the عذاب of the خسف and حاصب threatened in earlier ayahs.
- **هَذَا**: الإشارة (demonstrative) to the الوعد. Ibn Ashur observes that if the الوعد means the حشر, the إشارة is ظاهرة (transparent), referring back to وَإِلَيْهِ تُحْشَرُونَ. If it means a separate threat of نصر (victory for the Muslims), the إشارة points to a وعيد they had heard.
- **I'rab (Zuhayli):** هَذَا is مبتدأ (mubtada'), الْوَعْدُ is صفة (sifa) or بدل (badal), and مَتَى is the خبر (khabar), with a ضمير (pronoun) returning to الوعد.
- **Rhetorical device (Ibn Ashur):** The الاستفهام is مستعمل في التهكم (employed sarcastically). They used the word الوعد itself as a form of استنجاز, since a وعد inherently implies وفاء (fulfilment), and by asking مَتَى they mock the delay.

### Points of Divergence

**Issue: What is المراد بالوعد (the intended referent of "the promise")?**
- View 1 (majority, including Ibn Kathir, Qurtubi, Zuhayli): It refers to the حشر (resurrection) and البعث, drawing on وَإِلَيْهِ تُحْشَرُونَ immediately before it.
- View 2 (Zuhayli, as an alternative): It could refer to the عذاب الدنيا, specifically the threatened خسف and حاصب from ayahs 16-17.
- View 3 (Ibn Ashur): It may refer to a separate وعد بالنصر (promise of victory for the Muslims), with the إشارة pointing to a threat they had heard from the Prophet.

### Unique Insights

- **Maturidi** draws a remarkable epistemological argument: the Prophet's refusal to specify a date for the الوعد, when doing so would have been the easiest strategy for a fabricator, constitutes an internal proof of genuine revelation. A liar would assign a conveniently distant date; a true messenger says only what he has been told.
- **Ibn Ashur** connects the use of the مضارع tense (وَيَقُولُونَ) to their habit of constantly repeating this taunt, making it a characteristic behaviour rather than a one-time event, which intensifies the picture of their عناد (obstinacy).

## Ayah 26: قُلْ إِنَّمَا الْعِلْمُ عِنْدَ اللَّهِ وَإِنَّمَا أَنَا نَذِيرٌ مُبِينٌ

### Core Meaning

God commands the Prophet to respond to their mocking demand with two clarifications that define the boundaries of prophetic function. First, knowledge of the timing of the الوعد belongs exclusively to God: لَا يَعْلَمُ وَقْتَ ذَلِكَ عَلَى التَّعْيِينِ إِلَّا اللَّهُ (Ibn Kathir). Second, the Prophet's role is strictly that of a نذير مبين (a clear warner), not a custodian of الغيب (the unseen). He was commanded to inform them that the promise is كائن وواقع لا محالة (certain and inevitable), but the exact time is not part of his mandate (Ibn Kathir, Zuhayli). The response operates on the level of what Ibn Ashur calls الأسلوب الحكيم (the wise rhetorical technique): instead of engaging with their sarcastic intent, the answer addresses the literal surface of their question, thereby reframing the discussion on God's terms.

### Linguistic Analysis

- **إِنَّمَا**: أداة حصر (restrictive particle). Two قصر (restriction) constructions operate here. The first, إِنَّمَا الْعِلْمُ عِنْدَ اللَّهِ, is قصر حقيقي (real restriction): no one at all knows the time except God (Ibn Ashur). The second, وَإِنَّمَا أَنَا نَذِيرٌ مُبِينٌ, is قصر إضافي (relative/contrastive restriction): I am only a warner, not one who knows the وقت (timing) (Ibn Ashur).
- **الْعِلْمُ**: The definite article ال is لام العهد (the lam of anaphoric reference), i.e. العلم بوقت هذا الوعد (knowledge of the time of this promise). Ibn Ashur calls this the لام العوض عن المضاف إليه (the lam standing in for the genitive complement).
- **نَذِيرٌ**: either مصدر بمعنى اسم الفاعل (masdar functioning as an active participle, meaning منذر), or it is فعيل بمعنى فاعل directly. Qurtubi glosses it as مخوف ومعلم (one who frightens and informs).
- **مُبِينٌ**: اسم فاعل من أبان المتعدي (active participle of the transitive form), meaning مبيّن لما أمرت بتبليغه (one who makes clear what he was commanded to convey) (Ibn Ashur).
- **Rhetorical device (Ibn Ashur):** The الأسلوب الحكيم (wise style). They asked مَتَى with sarcastic intent; the answer treats the question as a genuine temporal inquiry about the الغيب, and pivots to the more important truth: the الوعد is certain, even if its timing is unknown.

### Points of Divergence

**Issue: Scope of قُلْ and its connection to the preceding discourse**
- View 1 (Ibn Ashur): The فعل قُلْ here is specifically an أمر بقول يختص بجواب كلامهم (a command to respond to their particular speech), and it is فُصِلَ (detached) without a conjunction because it falls within the سياق المحاورة (context of dialogue).
- View 2 (Zuhayli, Safwat): It is simply a direct divine command for the Prophet to convey God's answer, part of the standard أمر بالتبليغ (command to deliver the message).

### Unique Insights

- **Maturidi** develops a subtle argument about the two إنما clauses. He observes that the Prophet's statement لا أزيد في الإنذار على القدر الذي أمرت به (I do not add to the warning beyond what I was commanded) reveals the discipline of revelation: the true messenger transmits exactly what he receives, neither more nor less. This restraint itself is evidence of صدق (truthfulness).
- **Zuhayli** summarizes the verse's lesson in his فقه الحياة section: علم وقت قيام الساعة عند الله وحده، فلا يعلمه غيره. وما مهمة الرسول إلا البلاغ المبين والإنذار والتخويف البيّن من العذاب (knowledge of the Hour's timing belongs to God alone; the Messenger's task is solely clear communication and clear warning of punishment).

## Ayah 27: فَلَمَّا رَأَوْهُ زُلْفَةً سِيئَتْ وُجُوهُ الَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا وَقِيلَ هَذَا الَّذِي كُنْتُمْ بِهِ تَدَّعُونَ

### Core Meaning

This ayah shifts the timeline forward to the moment the الوعد is fulfilled, describing the reaction of the disbelievers when they finally see what they had mocked. The majority of مفسرون hold that the الضمير (pronoun) in رَأَوْهُ refers to the الوعد, and the الزلفة means القرب (nearness): they see it close at hand, bearing down on them (Zamakhshari, Zuhayli, Baghawi, Ibn Kathir). Their faces are overtaken by سوء (wretchedness): blackening, كآبة (gloom), and قترة (dust-like dullness) (Zamakhshari, Baghawi, Zuhayli). The angels of punishment, الخزنة or الزبانية, then address them with a devastating تقريع (rebuke): "This is what you used to demand" (Ibn Kathir, Zuhayli, Safwat).

The mufassirun agree that the past tense (رَأَوْهُ, سِيئَتْ, قِيلَ) is used for a future event to convey تحقق الوقوع (certainty of occurrence), as if it has already happened (Zuhayli, Ibn Ashur). Ibn Ashur provides the most detailed analysis of this temporal shift, comparing it to أَتَى أَمْرُ اللَّهِ (God's command has come) in Surat al-Nahl, where a future event is rendered in the past tense because it emanates from one whose words are infallible.

### Linguistic Analysis

- **زُلْفَةً**: مصدر أو اسم مصدر of زَلَفَ meaning قَرُبَ (Ibn Ashur). Zamakhshari offers two i'rab possibilities: it is either حال (hal, i.e. ذا زلفة, meaning "possessing nearness") or ظرف (zarf, i.e. مكانًا ذا زلفة, meaning "in a place of nearness"). Ibn Ashur reads it as إخبار بالمصدر للمبالغة (predication by masdar for hyperbole), meaning the العذاب is intensely, overwhelmingly close.
- **سِيئَتْ**: مبني للمجهول (passive voice) from ساءَ. The نائب الفاعل (deputy subject) is وُجُوهُ. The meaning is ساءَ الوعدُ وجوههم, i.e. the sight of the promise caused their faces wretchedness (Ibn Ashur). Qurtubi cites al-Zajjaj: تبين فيها السوء (the wretchedness became manifest on them), comparing it to يَوْمَ تَبْيَضُّ وُجُوهٌ وَتَسْوَدُّ وُجُوهٌ. Ibn Ashur adds that assigning سوء to the وجوه implies تضمين (semantic inclusion) of the meaning كَلَحَت (became grim-faced), since extreme distress manifests on faces.
- **تَدَّعُونَ**: The principal قراءة (reading) is with تشديد الدال (doubled dal), i.e. تَفْتَعِلُونَ from الدعاء, meaning تطلبون وتستعجلون (you demand and hasten) (Zamakhshari, Zuhayli, Qurtubi). The قراءة of Yaqub is تَدْعُونَ (with تخفيف), from simple الدعاء, and the two readings carry the same meaning (Baghawi, Qurtubi). An additional interpretation: it derives from الدعوى, meaning تدّعون أنه لا يكون (you claim it will never happen) (Zamakhshari, Ibn Ashur). Ibn Ashur combines both via تضمين, arguing that تَدَّعُونَ is ضُمِّنَ معنى تُكَذِّبُونَ (includes the meaning of "you deny"), which explains why the preposition بِهِ attaches to it.
- **الفاء الفصيحة (Ibn Ashur):** The فاء in فَلَمَّا is a فاء فصيحة (elucidative fa'), implying a suppressed clause: فَحَلَّ بِهِمُ الوَعْدُ فَلَمَّا رَأَوْهُ (so the promise befell them, and when they saw it...).
- **Qira'at (Qurtubi, Ibn Ashur):** سِيئَتْ is read by Nafi', Ibn Amir, and al-Kisa'i with إشمام (a hint of damma blended into the kasra on the sin), while the rest read it with a pure كسرة. Both are valid لغات (dialectal variants) for passive forms of ajwaf verbs (verbs with a weak middle radical).

### Points of Divergence

**Issue: What does الضمير in رَأَوْهُ refer to, and what moment is described?**
- View 1 (majority: Ibn Kathir, Zuhayli, Baghawi, Zamakhshari, Safwat, Maturidi): It refers to the عذاب الآخرة (punishment of the Hereafter); they see it on يوم القيامة.
- View 2 (Mujahid, cited by Baghawi and Qurtubi): It refers to عذاب بدر (the punishment at the Battle of Badr).
- View 3 (Ibn Abbas, cited by Qurtubi): They see their عمل السيئ (evil deeds) near and close.
- View 4 (Qurtubi, as a possibility): They see ما وعدوا من الحشر (what was promised of the resurrection) close to them.

**Issue: Who is the speaker in وَقِيلَ?**
- View 1 (Zamakhshari, Zuhayli, Safwat, Ibn Ashur): The الزبانية or الخزنة (angels of Hell).
- View 2 (Ibn Ashur, as alternative): ملائكة المحشر (angels of the gathering). He notes that the identity of the speaker was deliberately left unspecified because المقصود المقول دون القائل (the message matters more than the messenger), a form of إيجاز (conciseness).

### Unique Insights

- **Zamakhshari** transmits a moving anecdote: بعض الزهاد (one of the ascetics) recited this ayah at the beginning of his night prayer and kept repeating it, weeping, until the فجر call to prayer was made. Zamakhshari comments: لعمري إنها لوقاذة (by my life, it is devastating) for whoever truly envisions that scene. The word وقاذة (from وَقَذَ, to strike someone nearly to death) captures the overwhelming emotional impact of the ayah.
- **Ibn Ashur** provides a unique rhetorical reading of the القصر (restriction) in هَذَا الَّذِي كُنْتُمْ بِهِ تَدَّعُونَ. Because both parts of the nominal sentence (هذا and الذي) are definite (معرفة), the resulting قصر functions as تعريض (indirect accusation): they are so deep in جحود (denial) that even when they see the العذاب, they might mistake it for something else, much like the people of عاد who saw the approaching storm and said هَذَا عَارِضٌ مُمْطِرُنَا (this is a cloud bringing us rain).
- **Maturidi** emphasises the temporal-psychological dimension: when they finally see the أهوال (terrors), they realise that the time between the Prophet's warning and its fulfilment was in fact قريبة (near), even though in their worldly existence they had treated it as بعيدة (distant). He compares this to كَأَنَّهُمْ يَوْمَ يَرَوْنَهَا لَمْ يَلْبَثُوا إِلَّا عَشِيَّةً أَوْ ضُحَاهَا.

## Ayah 28: قُلْ أَرَأَيْتُمْ إِنْ أَهْلَكَنِيَ اللَّهُ وَمَنْ مَعِيَ أَوْ رَحِمَنَا فَمَنْ يُجِيرُ الْكَافِرِينَ مِنْ عَذَابٍ أَلِيمٍ

### Core Meaning

The disbelievers of Mecca used to wish for and anticipate the death of the Prophet and the destruction of the believers, as Quran reports elsewhere: أَمْ يَقُولُونَ شَاعِرٌ نَتَرَبَّصُ بِهِ رَيْبَ الْمَنُونِ (Qurtubi, Zamakhshari, Ibn Ashur, Zuhayli). God commands the Prophet to confront this malice with a devastating logical argument: whether God takes the believers' lives or extends them, either outcome changes nothing for the كافرين. Their doom is sealed by their own كفر, and no one can shield them from the عذاب الأليم (Ibn Kathir, Zuhayli).

The verse reframes the discussion: instead of asking about the Prophet's fate, they should be asking about their own. Ibn Kathir captures this with: خَلِّصوا أَنْفُسَكُمْ (save yourselves). Zuhayli identifies the المراد (intended message) as a call for the disbelievers to seek نجاة (salvation) through التوبة والإنابة والرجوع إلى الله بالإيمان (repentance, turning back, and returning to God through faith).

### Linguistic Analysis

- **أَرَأَيْتُمْ**: الرؤية علمية (cognitive seeing, not visual), i.e. أخبروني (tell me). The verb is معلّق عن العمل (suspended from governing) by the استفهام in the جواب الشرط (Ibn Ashur, Zuhayli).
- **أَهْلَكَنِيَ**: الإهلاك here means الإماتة (causing death) (Ibn Ashur, Qurtubi).
- **رَحِمَنَا**: The مقابلة (juxtaposition) of أَهْلَكَنِيَ and رَحِمَنَا indicates that the رحمة here means الحياة (life/survival). Ibn Ashur draws the theological conclusion that الحياة رحمة (life is a mercy) and تأخير الأجل من النعم (delaying the appointed time of death is a blessing).
- **الفاء in فَمَنْ يُجِيرُ** (Zuhayli): According to one view, the فاء is رابطة لجواب الشرط (linking the response to the conditional). According to another, it is جواب for the implicit meaning of أَرَأَيْتُمْ (which carries the meaning "pay attention"), so the structure is: انتبهوا فمن يجير.
- **الْكَافِرِينَ**: placed where one would expect a pronoun (يجيركم). Safwat and Ibn Ashur note this is إظهار في موضع الإضمار (using a noun where a pronoun was expected) for two purposes: تسجيل عليهم بالكفر (branding them with the label of كفر) and الإيماء إلى العلة (hinting at the reason for their doom, since the وصف of كفر itself explains the حكم of عذاب).
- **عَذَابٍ**: تنكير للتهويل (indefinite for the purpose of amplification/intimidation) (Ibn Ashur).

### Points of Divergence

**Issue: What is the meaning of the إهلاك/رحمة dichotomy?**
- View 1 (Ibn Kathir, Qurtubi, Zuhayli, Safwat): أَهْلَكَنِيَ = أماتني (caused my death); رَحِمَنَا = أخّر آجالنا (extended our lives). The point: whether we die or live, you are still doomed.
- View 2 (Zamakhshari, first interpretation): إن أهلكنا كما تتمنون فننقلب إلى الجنة (if we perish as you wish, we go to Paradise); أو رحمنا بالنصرة والإدالة للإسلام (or if we are granted mercy through victory and the triumph of Islam), then either way, you gain nothing.
- View 3 (Zamakhshari, second interpretation): If God takes us through death, فمن يجيركم بعد موت هداتكم والآخذين بحجزكم من النار (who will protect you after the death of your guides who were holding you back from the Fire)?
- View 4 (Zamakhshari, third interpretation): If God destroys us in the آخرة because of our ذنوب and we are مسلمون, فمن يجير من لا إيمان له (who will protect those with no faith at all)?
- View 5 (Baghawi, citing Ibn Abbas): Even with our إيمان, we are خائفون (fearful) that God might destroy us for our sins, since His حكم is نافذ (decisive). How much worse, then, for the كافرون?

**Issue: Theological implications of the verse**
- **Maturidi** draws a major عقدي (creedal) conclusion: the verse proves that God has the right to punish even for صغائر (minor sins), because the Prophet and his companions were معصومون عن الكبائر (protected from major sins), yet the verse contemplates their إهلاك. This, in turn, refutes the المعتزلة position that God cannot punish for small sins. It also establishes the مشيئة المغفرة (divine prerogative of forgiveness) for believers who commit sins short of كفر, while confirming that كفر itself cannot be forgiven, as stated in إِنَّ اللَّهَ لَا يَغْفِرُ أَنْ يُشْرَكَ بِهِ.

### Unique Insights

- **Zamakhshari** offers three distinct interpretive layers for a single verse, each yielding a different argument against the disbelievers, from the eschatological to the pragmatic.
- **Ibn Ashur** connects this verse to a broader Quranic pattern: God repeatedly addresses the disbelievers' obsession with the Prophet's death (citing وَمَا جَعَلْنَا لِبَشَرٍ مِنْ قَبْلِكَ الْخُلْدَ and إِنَّكَ مَيِّتٌ وَإِنَّهُمْ مَيِّتُونَ), then subverts it by showing that the Prophet's death does not rescue them from عذاب.
- **Maturidi** extends his theological argument across an entire page, contending that the Khawarij and Mu'tazila, who deny that God forgives كبائر, ironically place themselves beyond the scope of God's mercy, because if God were to forgive them, they would attribute it to the smallness of their sins rather than to divine grace, thereby nullifying the very meaning of عفو.

## Ayah 29: قُلْ هُوَ الرَّحْمَنُ آمَنَّا بِهِ وَعَلَيْهِ تَوَكَّلْنَا فَسَتَعْلَمُونَ مَنْ هُوَ فِي ضَلَالٍ مُبِينٍ

### Core Meaning

The Prophet is commanded to declare the identity of the God he worships: هُوَ الرَّحْمَنُ (He is al-Rahman), to affirm the believers' إيمان (faith) in Him, and to assert their exclusive توكل (reliance) on Him alone. The verse then concludes with a promise/threat: فَسَتَعْلَمُونَ مَنْ هُوَ فِي ضَلَالٍ مُبِينٍ (you will come to know who is in manifest error). This operates as a تهديد (threat), leaving the identification of the ضال (the one in error) formally open, though every clue in the context points to the disbelievers (Zuhayli, Ibn Ashur).

The choice of the divine name الرَّحْمَنُ carries particular weight. The Meccan pagans found this name offensive and rejected it (cf. وَإِذَا قِيلَ لَهُمُ اسْجُدُوا لِلرَّحْمَنِ قَالُوا وَمَا الرَّحْمَنُ). Ibn Ashur observes that by affirming الرحمن, the believers position themselves as recipients of رحمة, while those who denied this name become أحرياء بأن يحرموا آثار رحمته (deserving of being deprived of the effects of His mercy).

### Linguistic Analysis

- **Word order of آمَنَّا بِهِ vs. وَعَلَيْهِ تَوَكَّلْنَا**: Zamakhshari raises the question: why is the مفعول (object) of آمنا postposed (بِهِ after the verb), while the مفعول of توكلنا is preposed (عَلَيْهِ before the verb)? He answers: آمنا was placed first as a تعريض (indirect jab) at the كافرين, since it follows immediately after the mention of their كفر (فَمَنْ يُجِيرُ الْكَافِرِينَ). The primary message is: we believed, and you did not. Then عَلَيْهِ تَوَكَّلْنَا is preposed with تقديم المعمول (fronting the complement) to express الاختصاص (exclusivity): we rely on Him alone, not on what you rely on, namely your رجال (men) and أموال (wealth) (Zamakhshari, Qurtubi, Ibn Ashur).
- Ibn Ashur expands: the non-fronting of بِهِ in آمَنَّا بِهِ is deliberate. The point of آمنا is not to say "we believe in Him alone" (which would require fronting), but rather "we believed, period" (contrasting with their كفر). The exclusivity emphasis is reserved for the التوكل clause, because توكل involves choosing a protector/helper, and the mushrikun had made their أصنام their protectors.
- **فَسَتَعْلَمُونَ مَنْ هُوَ فِي ضَلَالٍ مُبِينٍ**: مَنْ is موصولة (relative pronoun), not استفهامية. The referent is formally مبهم (ambiguous), oscillating between the two فريقان (groups): the Prophet's followers and the كافرين. But every contextual indicator reveals that the ضالّون are those who جَحَدُوا وَصْفَ الرَّحْمَنُ وَتَوَكَّلُوا عَلَى الْأَوْثَانِ (denied the name al-Rahman and relied on idols) (Ibn Ashur).
- **Qira'at (Qurtubi, Baghawi, Ibn Ashur):** The majority read فَسَتَعْلَمُونَ with تاء الخطاب (second-person ta'), as part of the Prophet's address to the disbelievers. Al-Kisa'i reads it with ياء الغيبة (third-person ya'), making it God's statement about them to the Prophet, i.e. an إخبار بأنه سيعاقبهم (a divine promise that He will punish them) (Ibn Ashur).

### Points of Divergence

**Issue: What does هُوَ refer to, and what is the syntactic role of الرَّحْمَنُ?**
- View 1 (Ibn Ashur): هُوَ is a pronoun referring back to الله in the preceding context; الرَّحْمَنُ is the خبر (predicate). The meaning: the God who could destroy us or show us mercy is the الرحمن. This highlights that the believers are connected to the وصف of رحمة.
- View 2 (Maturidi): هُوَ could be an اسم from the أسماء الله (a divine name in its own right), paralleling the discussion in Surat al-Ikhlas. الرحمن would then be a second name, and the sentence links two divine names: هو والرحمن.
- View 3 (Baghawi): هُوَ الرَّحْمَنُ الَّذِي نَعْبُدُهُ (He is al-Rahman whom we worship), a simple identification statement.

### Unique Insights

- **Ibn Ashur** provides a layered analysis of why the Meccans' rejection of the name الرحمن is theologically self-destructive: by refusing this name, they sever themselves from the very attribute (رحمة) that could have saved them. The believers, by affirming it, place themselves under its protection.
- **Maturidi** reads the verse as a comprehensive عقدي (creedal) declaration. He understands قُلْ هُوَ الرَّحْمَنُ as an affirmation that the خالق of everything described in the surah (الموت والحياة, السماوات, الأرض, الطير, السمع والبصر والأفئدة) is الرحمن, the one who creates through رحمة, not through random force or caprice.

## Ayah 30: قُلْ أَرَأَيْتُمْ إِنْ أَصْبَحَ مَاؤُكُمْ غَوْرًا فَمَنْ يَأْتِيكُمْ بِمَاءٍ مَعِينٍ

### Core Meaning

The surah closes with a powerful rhetorical challenge that brings the argument from the cosmic down to the viscerally immediate. After establishing God's sovereignty over creation, resurrection, and the unseen, the final verse asks the disbelievers to consider their most basic daily dependency: water. If your water were to vanish into the depths of the earth, who could bring you flowing, accessible water other than God? (Ibn Kathir, Zuhayli, Qurtubi).

This connects backwards to أَمَّنْ هَذَا الَّذِي يَرْزُقُكُمْ إِنْ أَمْسَكَ رِزْقَهُ (ayah 21), forming an inclusio that brackets the entire argument: no one provides, sustains, or rescues except الله (Maturidi). Ibn Ashur adds a geographical specificity: Mecca was a city of extremely limited water sources, dependent primarily on بئر زمزم and بئر ميمون (Qurtubi confirms both wells by name). The threat of غور (water sinking away) was therefore not abstract but existentially immediate.

The مفسرون universally agree that the expected answer is لَا يَأْتِيكُمْ بِهِ إِلَّا اللَّهُ (none can bring it except God), and even the disbelievers would be forced to concede this. The implicit follow-up, as Qurtubi and Zuhayli state: فَلِمَ تُشْرِكُونَ بِهِ مَنْ لَا يَقْدِرُ عَلَى أَنْ يَأْتِيَكُمْ (then why do you associate with Him those who cannot bring it to you?).

### Linguistic Analysis

- **غَوْرًا**: مصدر وُصِفَ بِهِ (a masdar used as an adjective), meaning غائرًا (sunken, withdrawn into the depths of the earth). The وصف بالمصدر is for المبالغة (hyperbole), like عَدْل and رِضًا (Zamakhshari, Ibn Ashur, Qurtubi). Al-Kalbi glosses it: لا تناله الدلاء (not reachable by buckets) (Zamakhshari, Baghawi). Ibn Kathir adds: لَا يُنَال بِالْفُئُوسِ الْحِدَادِ وَلَا السَّوَاعِدِ الشِّدَادِ (not reachable by sharp pickaxes or strong arms). الغائر is the عكس (opposite) of النابع (the springing/gushing) (Ibn Kathir).
- **أَصْبَحَ**: a ناقصة (deficient verb), with مَاؤُكُمْ as its اسم and غَوْرًا as its خبر (Zuhayli).
- **مَعِينٍ**: Two morphological analyses are offered:
  - فَعِيل from مَعَنَ الماء (the water flowed abundantly), with the ميم as أصلية (radical). Meaning: ماء كثير جارٍ (abundant flowing water) (Zuhayli, Qurtubi).
  - مَفْعُول from العين (the eye/spring), originally مَعْيُون, with phonological simplification (the damma on the ya' was dropped, then the waw was elided because of the preceding sukun). Meaning: ماء ظاهر تراه العيون (water visible to the eye) (Ibn Abbas via Baghawi, Zuhayli, Qurtubi). Ibn Abbas also offers a third meaning: ماء جارٍ (running water) (Baghawi) or ماء عذب (sweet water) (Qurtubi).
- **Parallel verse (Zuhayli):** أَفَرَأَيْتُمُ الْمَاءَ الَّذِي تَشْرَبُونَ أَأَنْتُمْ أَنْزَلْتُمُوهُ مِنَ الْمُزْنِ أَمْ نَحْنُ الْمُنْزِلُونَ (al-Waqi'a 56:68-69).

### Points of Divergence

**Issue: What specific water is meant?**
- View 1 (al-Kalbi, Muqatil, cited by Baghawi): It refers specifically to ماء زمزم.
- View 2 (Qurtubi): The water of بئر زمزم and بئر ميمون, the two wells of Mecca.
- View 3 (Ibn Ashur): The آبار (wells) of Mecca generally, noting the city's extreme scarcity of water before and during the Prophet's time, with only زمزم and possibly بئر ميمون and الجفر.
- View 4 (General interpretation, Ibn Kathir, Zuhayli): All water on earth, including rivers, springs, and wells. God's فضل وكرم (bounty and generosity) is manifest in providing water across all أقطار الأرض (regions of the earth).

### Unique Insights

- **Zamakhshari** and **Zuhayli** both record the famous cautionary tale: بعض الشطار (a certain brazen individual, identified in some sources as محمد بن زكريا الطبيب according to Ibn Ashur's citation of the Kashshaf's supplementary notes) heard this verse recited and responded with arrogant defiance: تَجِيءُ بِهِ الْفُؤُوسُ وَالْمَعَاوِلُ (the pickaxes and shovels will bring it). As divine retribution, فَذَهَبَ مَاءُ عَيْنَيْهِ (the water of his eyes departed, i.e. he went blind). The word play between مَاءٍ مَعِينٍ and مَاءُ العينين adds a layer of poetic justice that the mufassirun find significant.
- **Ibn Ashur** uniquely connects this verse to the actual قحط (famine/drought) that struck Mecca after the Prophet's migration to Medina, referenced in Surat al-Dukhan. He notes the hydrological principle that well water depends on rainfall: انحباس المطر يتبعه غور مياه الآبار لأن استمدادها من الماء النازل على الأرض (cessation of rain is followed by sinking of well water because their supply comes from water descending on the earth), citing أَلَمْ تَرَ أَنَّ اللَّهَ أَنْزَلَ مِنَ السَّمَاءِ مَاءً فَسَلَكَهُ يَنَابِيعَ فِي الْأَرْضِ (al-Zumar 39:21).
- **Maturidi** reads the verse as a direct continuation of ayah 21 (أَمَّنْ هَذَا الَّذِي يَرْزُقُكُمْ إِنْ أَمْسَكَ رِزْقَهُ), creating a structural frame for the surah's closing argument. The logic is: you already admitted (implicitly) that no one provides your رزق except God; now admit the same about your ماء, the most essential component of رزق.
- **Zuhayli** identifies the verse as دليل على وجوب الاعتماد على الله تعالى في كل حاجة (proof of the obligation to rely on God in every need), and adds that it is also an إشارة إلى أن الفتوح العقلي لا يتيسر إلا بإعانة الله تعالى (a hint that intellectual and spiritual openings are only possible through God's assistance), extending the water metaphor from the physical to the spiritual domain.
- **Zuhayli** concludes: it is recommended (يُستحب) that the reader say الله رب العالمين after reciting بِمَاءٍ مَعِينٍ, as reported in hadith tradition.
