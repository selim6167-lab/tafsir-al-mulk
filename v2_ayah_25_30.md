# Surah Al-Mulk (67): Ayahs 25-30

## The Closing Movement: An Unfinished Test

The surah opened with an announcement: الذي خلق الموت والحياة ليبلوكم أيكم أحسن عملا. Life is a test. Everything that followed, the flawless heavens, the groaning earth, the birds held aloft, the senses you were given, built the case for the Examiner's authority. Now, in these final six ayahs, the surah closes not by resolving the test but by dramatising it. The disbelievers demand to know the test results before the exam is over. The Prophet is told to refuse the demand, to declare his own position within the test, and to model the correct response: faith and reliance. Then the surah ends with a question, not an answer, because the test is still running.

---

## Ayah 25

> وَيَقُولُونَ مَتَى هَذَا الْوَعْدُ إِنْ كُنْتُمْ صَادِقِينَ

The question متى هذا الوعد is not a question at all. It is a taunt wearing the grammar of inquiry, استهزاء dressed as استفهام (Zuhayli, Maturidi). They have just been told وإليه تحشرون (to Him you will be gathered), and their response is to demand a date, as though the Resurrection were an appointment they could pencil into a calendar.

Ibn Ashur notices something in the verb tense that sharpens the picture. God reports their words with the مضارع (present continuous): ويقولون, not قالوا. This is not something they said once. It is something they keep saying, a habitual refrain, a settled posture of عناد (obstinacy). The tense marks it as a defining characteristic, not a momentary outburst.

The word الوعد itself is weaponised. A وعد implies وفاء (fulfilment), so by asking متى they are mocking the delay: you promised, so where is it? (Ibn Ashur). It can refer to the حشر (resurrection), the عذاب threatened in earlier ayahs (the خسف and the حاصب), or even a promise of Muslim victory (Ibn Ashur offers all three). The ambiguity does not matter much, because the response in the next ayah covers all of them equally.

Maturidi draws an argument from this verse that runs against the grain of mockery. Their taunt, he observes, is paradoxically a proof of the Prophet's sincerity. A fabricator inventing a religion would assign his promises to a conveniently distant future, safely beyond his own lifetime. The Prophet does no such thing. He says: إنما العلم عند الله. He refuses to fill in the blank, because he transmits only what he has been given. The restraint is the evidence.

## Ayah 26

> قُلْ إِنَّمَا الْعِلْمُ عِنْدَ اللَّهِ وَإِنَّمَا أَنَا نَذِيرٌ مُبِينٌ

The response is a masterpiece of الأسلوب الحكيم (the wise rhetorical technique, as Ibn Ashur names it): instead of engaging with the sarcasm behind متى, the answer treats the question at face value and redirects it. You asked when. The answer: only God knows when. But the when is not your problem. The whether is.

Two إنما clauses divide the territory with surgical precision. The first, إنما العلم عند الله, is قصر حقيقي (real restriction): no one at all, not angel nor prophet, knows the timing (Ibn Ashur). The ال on العلم is عهدية, referring specifically to the knowledge of the timing they asked about (Ibn Ashur). The second, وإنما أنا نذير مبين, is قصر إضافي (relative restriction): I am a warner, not a scheduler. My job description has boundaries, and predicting dates is outside them.

Maturidi adds a point about prophetic discipline that completes the picture. The Prophet's statement amounts to: لا أزيد في الإنذار على القدر الذي أمرت به (I do not add to the warning beyond what I was commanded). A true messenger transmits exactly what he receives, no more and no less. The very refusal to speculate is proof of authentic revelation.

The word مبين (from أبان, the transitive form) means he makes clear what he was commanded to convey (Ibn Ashur). Clarity of warning is not the same as knowledge of outcomes. The نذير makes the danger visible. He does not control the timeline.

## Ayah 27

> فَلَمَّا رَأَوْهُ زُلْفَةً سِيئَتْ وُجُوهُ الَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا وَقِيلَ هَذَا الَّذِي كُنْتُمْ بِهِ تَدَّعُونَ

The tense shifts, and suddenly we are in the future as though it has already happened. They asked متى. Now the Quran shows them their own faces at the moment of متى's answer.

The فاء in فلما is a فاء فصيحة (elucidative fa'), implying a compressed narrative: the promise arrived, and when they saw it... (Ibn Ashur). The past tense for a future event is deliberate. It signals تحقق الوقوع (certainty of occurrence): what comes from God is as good as done. Ibn Ashur compares it to أتى أمر الله, where a future event is rendered in the past because it emanates from one whose words cannot fail.

The word زلفة (nearness) strikes hard. It is a مصدر used as a predicate for المبالغة (hyperbole): they saw it intensely, overwhelmingly close (Ibn Ashur). Not approaching, not on the horizon, but upon them. Zamakhshari reads it as either حال (a state: "possessing nearness") or ظرف (a place: "in a place of nearness"). Either way, the distance they imagined between themselves and the promise collapses to zero.

Then سيئت وجوه الذين كفروا. Their faces are overtaken by سوء: blackening, كآبة (gloom), قترة (ashen dullness) (Zamakhshari, Baghawi, Zuhayli). Ibn Ashur notes that assigning the سوء to the وجوه implies تضمين (semantic inclusion) of the meaning كلحت (became grim-faced), because extreme internal distress surfaces on faces before anything else.

And then the rebuke: وقيل هذا الذي كنتم به تدعون. The speakers are the زبانية or the خزنة (angels of punishment) (Zamakhshari, Zuhayli), though Ibn Ashur notes that the Quran deliberately leaves the speaker unidentified because المقصود المقول دون القائل (the message matters, not the messenger). The word تدعون (from ادّعى) means you used to demand, to claim it would never happen, to call for it in mockery (Zamakhshari, Ibn Ashur). Ibn Ashur adds that the verb carries a تضمين (embedded meaning) of تكذبون (you used to deny), which is why the preposition به attaches to it.

Ibn Ashur identifies a further layer in the rhetoric. Both parts of the sentence, هذا and الذي, are definite (معرفة), producing a قصر (restriction) that functions as تعريض (indirect accusation): they are so deep in denial that even when they see the punishment, they might mistake it for something else, like the people of عاد who saw the approaching storm cloud and said: هذا عارض ممطرنا (this is rain coming for us). The angels have to point and say: no, this is what you asked for.

Maturidi captures the temporal-psychological dimension. When they finally see the أهوال (terrors), they realise the interval between the Prophet's warning and its fulfilment was in fact قريبة (near), even though in worldly time they treated it as بعيدة (distant). He compares this to كأنهم يوم يرونها لم يلبثوا إلا عشية أو ضحاها: on the day they see it, it will feel as though they had waited only an afternoon.

Zamakhshari transmits a small, devastating anecdote. One of the زهاد (ascetics) began reciting this ayah in his night prayer, then could not move past it. He kept repeating it, weeping, until the call for fajr was made. Zamakhshari comments: لعمري إنها لوقاذة (by my life, it is devastating) for whoever truly envisions that scene. The word وقاذة, from وقذ (to strike someone nearly to death), says everything.

## Ayah 28

> قُلْ أَرَأَيْتُمْ إِنْ أَهْلَكَنِيَ اللَّهُ وَمَنْ مَعِيَ أَوْ رَحِمَنَا فَمَنْ يُجِيرُ الْكَافِرِينَ مِنْ عَذَابٍ أَلِيمٍ

The surah now does something structurally remarkable. After telling the disbelievers that the promise is certain and its timing belongs to God alone, it turns inward. The Prophet is told to place himself and the believers within the same framework of divine will. Whether God destroys us or shows us mercy, what difference does that make to your situation?

The Meccan pagans used to wish openly for the Prophet's death. The Quran records their sentiment elsewhere: شاعر نتربص به ريب المنون, a poet, we will wait for time to finish him off (Ibn Ashur, Qurtubi, Zamakhshari). This ayah dismantles the logic of that wish. Your fate is sealed by your own كفر. The Prophet's death would not rescue you, and his survival does not threaten you any more than your own disbelief already does. Ibn Kathir puts it in four words: خلّصوا أنفسكم (save yourselves).

The opposition of أهلكني and رحمنا is itself a theological statement. Ibn Ashur draws out the implication: the مقابلة (juxtaposition) tells you that الحياة رحمة (life is a mercy). To live is to receive رحمة, and تأخير الأجل (the deferral of one's appointed death) is among God's blessings. This is not incidental phrasing. It is a lens through which to read the Prophet's own mortality, which the surah does not pretend to deny.

Zamakhshari offers three distinct readings of the verse, each yielding a different argument. First: if we perish as you wish, we go to Paradise; if God grants us victory, Islam prevails. Either way, you gain nothing. Second: if we die, who will guide you then? You lose your last chance at salvation. Third: if even we, as Muslims, face divine scrutiny for our sins, how much worse for those with no faith at all? The three readings radiate outward from the same core: the disbelievers' obsession with the Prophet's fate is a distraction from their own peril.

Baghawi, citing Ibn Abbas, takes the most psychologically honest reading. Even with our إيمان, he says, we are خائفون (fearful) that God might destroy us for our sins, because His حكم is نافذ (decisive). If believers with faith tremble before God's judgement, what shelter do the faithless imagine they have?

The word الكافرين appears where a pronoun (يجيركم) was expected. This is إظهار في موضع الإضمار (a noun where a pronoun belongs), and both Safwat and Ibn Ashur identify its purpose: تسجيل عليهم بالكفر (branding them with the label of disbelief) and الإيماء إلى العلة (hinting at the cause of their doom). The description is the verdict. They will not be protected because they are الكافرين, and كفر is the very thing from which no protection is available.

What makes this ayah structurally significant within the surah's ابتلاء framework is that the Prophet himself is placed inside the test. He does not stand above it as a judge or outside it as an observer. He is tested too. The difference between him and the disbelievers is not exemption from divine will but response to it.

## Ayah 29

> قُلْ هُوَ الرَّحْمَنُ آمَنَّا بِهِ وَعَلَيْهِ تَوَكَّلْنَا فَسَتَعْلَمُونَ مَنْ هُوَ فِي ضَلَالٍ مُبِينٍ

After placing himself within the test (ayah 28), the Prophet now demonstrates the correct answer. This is what it looks like to pass the examination that the surah announced in its second ayah. It has three components: identification of God (هو الرحمن), faith (آمنا به), and reliance (وعليه توكلنا).

The choice of the divine name الرحمن is not decorative. The Meccan pagans specifically rejected this name, as recorded elsewhere: وإذا قيل لهم اسجدوا للرحمن قالوا وما الرحمن. Ibn Ashur draws out the consequence: by affirming الرحمن, the believers position themselves as recipients of رحمة, while those who denied this name have severed themselves from the very attribute that could save them. They are أحرياء بأن يحرموا آثار رحمته (deserving of being deprived of the effects of His mercy). The name is not just theology. It is strategy.

Maturidi reads the verse as a comprehensive creedal declaration that gathers up the entire surah. هو الرحمن: the one who created الموت والحياة, the seven heavens, the ذلول earth, the birds in flight, the سمع والبصر والأفئدة. All of it is the work of الرحمن, the one who creates through رحمة, not through random force. The ayah retrospectively identifies the God behind every sign the surah has presented.

The word order in the two faith-clauses is Zamakhshari's finest observation in this passage. In آمنا به, the object (به) follows the verb. In وعليه توكلنا, the object (عليه) precedes it. Why? Because آمنا arrives as a تعريض (indirect jab) at the disbelievers: it follows immediately after the mention of their كفر (فمن يجير الكافرين). The primary message is: we believed, and you did not. The emphasis falls on the act of believing, not on its object. But عليه توكلنا uses تقديم المعمول (fronting the complement) to express الاختصاص (exclusivity): we rely on Him alone, not on what you rely on, your رجال (men) and أموال (wealth) (Zamakhshari, Qurtubi, Ibn Ashur).

Ibn Ashur extends this with a further precision. The non-fronting of به in آمنا به is deliberate. If the point were "we believe in Him alone" (excluding other objects of belief), the complement would have been fronted. But the point is simpler and sharper: we believed, period, in contrast to your كفر. The exclusivity claim is reserved for the توكل clause, because توكل involves choosing a protector, and the mushrikun had made their أصنام their protectors.

The verse closes with a threat wrapped in studied ambiguity: فستعلمون من هو في ضلال مبين. The word من is موصولة (relative pronoun, not interrogative), and its referent is formally مبهم (ambiguous), oscillating between two parties (Ibn Ashur). But every contextual signal, the denial of الرحمن, the reliance on idols, the obsession with the Prophet's death, points one way. The ambiguity is itself a rhetorical act. It forces the listener to work out the answer, which is an act of self-confrontation.

## Ayah 30

> قُلْ أَرَأَيْتُمْ إِنْ أَصْبَحَ مَاؤُكُمْ غَوْرًا فَمَنْ يَأْتِيكُمْ بِمَاءٍ مَعِينٍ

The surah ends with a question. Not a declaration, not a judgement, not a closing formula. A question, and the question is about water.

After the cosmic sweep of the preceding twenty-nine ayahs (sovereignty, death, the seven heavens, hell, birds, the earth, hearing, sight, resurrection), the final challenge drops to the most elemental human dependency. If your water sank into the earth tomorrow, who would bring it back? The descent from the cosmic to the visceral is the point. You cannot abstract your way out of needing water.

The word غورا is a مصدر used as an adjective (meaning غائرا, sunken), a construction that conveys المبالغة (hyperbole), like عدل and رضا (Zamakhshari, Ibn Ashur, Qurtubi). It means not merely reduced but gone: لا تناله الدلاء (beyond the reach of buckets, al-Kalbi via Zamakhshari), لا ينال بالفؤوس الحداد ولا السواعد الشداد (unreachable by sharp pickaxes or strong arms, Ibn Kathir). The opposite of غائر is نابع (springing forth), and the point is that you cannot manufacture the transition from one to the other.

Ibn Ashur adds geographical specificity that intensifies the threat. Mecca was a city of extremely limited water. Before زمزم, there were no wells and no springs. Even in the Prophet's time, the city depended on زمزم and perhaps بئر ميمون and one or two other wells. The threat of غور was not a philosophical exercise. It was an existential scenario that every Meccan listener could feel in the body.

Maturidi identifies the structural function of this closing. It connects directly back to ayah 21: أمّن هذا الذي يرزقكم إن أمسك رزقه (who is it that provides for you if He withholds His provision?). The surah asked that question earlier in the abstract. Now it asks it in the concrete: not "provision" in general, but water, the one provision without which all others are meaningless. The argument tightens from the general to the particular, from رزق to ماء.

The expected answer to فمن يأتيكم بماء معين is, of course, لا يأتيكم به إلا الله (none can bring it except God). Even the disbelievers would be forced to concede this. The implicit follow-up, as Qurtubi and Zuhayli note: then why do you associate with Him those who cannot bring it to you?

But the surah does not supply the answer. It ends on the question. This is structurally significant and should not be passed over quickly. The surah that announced in its second ayah that life is a test (ليبلوكم أيكم أحسن عملا) closes by leaving the test open. The question hangs in the air: فمن يأتيكم بماء معين? The listener must answer it, and in answering it, must confront what that answer means for everything else they believe. The test is still running. It runs for every listener in every age.

Zamakhshari and Zuhayli both record a cautionary tale that drives this home. A man (identified in some sources as محمد بن زكريا الطبيب, according to notes on the Kashshaf cited by Ibn Ashur) heard this verse recited and responded with arrogant defiance: تجيء به الفؤوس والمعاول (pickaxes and shovels will bring it). The divine retribution: فذهب ماء عينيه (the water of his eyes departed, and he went blind). The wordplay between ماء معين and ماء العينين is a layer of poetic justice that the mufassirun find deeply significant. He refused to answer God's question with humility, so the question answered itself upon him.

Zuhayli concludes that it is مستحب (recommended) for the reader to say الله رب العالمين after reciting بماء معين. The tradition is an acknowledgement that the surah's final question, though left open in the text, has only one answer.
