# Surah Al-Mulk (67): Ayahs 19-24

## Ayah 19

> أَوَلَمْ يَرَوْا إِلَى الطَّيْرِ فَوْقَهُمْ صَافَّاتٍ وَيَقْبِضْنَ مَا يُمْسِكُهُنَّ إِلَّا الرَّحْمَنُ إِنَّهُ بِكُلِّ شَيْءٍ بَصِيرٌ

The surah has just reminded the listener of destroyed nations and threatened the present ones with خسف and حاصب. Now, abruptly, it lifts the gaze upward. Look at the birds above you. The transition is deliberate: from the earth that could swallow you, to creatures that defy the earth altogether, suspended in a medium that has no business holding weight. Air is not like ground or sky (Maturidi): it is not a surface that bears things. That birds remain aloft despite their heaviness is, in itself, the proof.

Within the ابتلاء framework of the surah, the birds function as evidence presented to the test-taker. The surah has been building a case: the sky is flawless (ayah 3), the earth is made walkable (ayah 15), past nations were destroyed for failing the test (ayah 18). Now comes a different kind of evidence: not a threat, but a sign of لطف. The God who holds birds from plummeting to their death (هُوِيّ) is the same God who destroyed the أمم السابقة (Ibn Ashur). Had those nations not committed شرك and had they clung to obedience, He would have saved them from destruction just as He saves the birds from falling. The proof cuts both ways: divine power that sustains and divine power that destroys are one and the same power.

The language of the ayah is more expansive than its parallel in Surat al-Nahl (16:79), which says simply مسخرات في جو السماء. Here, three additional descriptive elements appear: فوقهم, صافّات, and يقبضن. Ibn Ashur explains why: the Quraysh did not heed the concise version, so the fuller description was given to break through their habituation. He offers a striking analogy: a Tunisian who first sees an elephant as an adult perceives its wonder more keenly than someone from India who grew up among elephants. People overlook the miracle of bird flight because they have watched it since infancy.

The most precise observation in the ayah belongs to the shift from the nominal صافّات to the verbal يقبضن. Zamakhshari explains: spreading the wings is the أصل (primary state) of flight, comparable to extending the limbs in swimming. Since it is the stable, continuous condition, it receives the اسم الفاعل form (indicating ثبات, permanence). The قبض, by contrast, is طارئ (intermittent, secondary), done for الاستظهار على التحرك (gaining momentum against gravity). So it receives the فعل المضارع form (indicating تجدّد, renewal). The grammar mirrors the physics: one form for the steady state, another for the dynamic burst.

On the closing word بصير: Ibn Ashur makes a distinction that matters. This is not بصير from the divine attribute السميع البصير (hearing and seeing), but بصير derived from بصيرة (insight), meaning عليم: He knows how to create and how to engineer wonders. The word is خبر (a predicate stating a fact), not وصف (a theological attribute in the technical sense). God's بصيرة is what produced the ten primary feathers (القوادم), the secondary feathers (الخوافي), the lightness of bones, the strength of the thoracic muscles (الجوانح), and the aerodynamic shape of the body (Ibn Ashur, Zamakhshari). These are not invisible strings suspending a conjurer's props; they are intrinsic design.

The choice of the name الرحمن here, rather than الله (which appears in the parallel verse in al-Nahl), is deliberate. The Quraysh specifically rejected this name, and the surah uses it four times as a pointed correction (Ibn Ashur). The name also serves the argument: it is the Merciful, specifically, who holds birds aloft, because cosmic design that prevents creatures from falling is itself an act of رحمة.

## Ayah 20

> أَمَّنْ هَذَا الَّذِي هُوَ جُنْدٌ لَكُمْ يَنْصُرُكُمْ مِنْ دُونِ الرَّحْمَنِ إِنِ الْكَافِرُونَ إِلَّا فِي غُرُورٍ

The surah shifts from evidence to interrogation. Having presented the proof (the birds, the sky, the earth), it now poses the first of two test questions directly to the listener. The أم is منقطعة (transitional): after establishing God's sole power over creation, the Quran turns to demolish the idea that anyone else could protect you from Him (Ibn Ashur).

The question is devastating in its structure. The اسم الإشارة (هذا) forces the listener to point at something concrete: name your army. Ibn Ashur reads it as pointing to a مفهوم مفروض في الأذهان, a hypothetical army summoned into the listener's imagination so it can be challenged. "Point to him and say: this is the one who will defend you." The استفهام is used for تعجيز (rendering incapable of response), and the impossibility of answering is the point. Zamakhshari offers a second reading: هذا may point directly to the أوثان themselves, the idols the mushrikin actually worshipped, believing they would shield them. On this reading, the question is even more cutting, because the listener must defend the proposition that stone statues constitute a military force.

The use of the nominal sentence هو جند لكم (rather than a verbal construction) implies دوام and ثبوت (permanence): a جند must be in a constant state of readiness, not just present during battle (Ibn Ashur). The idols fail even this minimal criterion.

Maturidi identifies the deepest irony. The mushrikin themselves knew their idols could not help in moments of real crisis, because when hardship struck, they turned to God alone: وإذا مس الإنسان ضر دعا ربه منيبا إليه. In adversity they conceded what they denied in prosperity. The ayah simply reminds them, in a time of ease (الأمن), of what they already know in a time of fear (الخوف).

The closing إن الكافرون إلا في غرور is an اعتراض (parenthetical) that generalizes beyond the immediate audience: all كافرون, in every time, are enveloped in غرور (Ibn Ashur). The word غرور means the soul's assumption that something beneficial will occur based on illusory indications. The ظرفية (في غرور) is مجازية: they are not merely experiencing delusion, they are inside it, surrounded on all sides, with no exit to الحق (Ibn Ashur).

Zuhayli adds a quiet observation about the phrase من دون الرحمن: the fact that mankind continues to exist despite their كفر is itself an act of رحمة from الرحمن. The name rebukes them even as it sustains them.

## Ayah 21

> أَمَّنْ هَذَا الَّذِي يَرْزُقُكُمْ إِنْ أَمْسَكَ رِزْقَهُ بَلْ لَجُّوا فِي عُتُوٍّ وَنُفُورٍ

The second test question. If ayah 20 challenged the listener on قوة (protection), this one challenges on رزق (sustenance). Together they strip away the two things any would-be deity must provide: security and provision. The structure mirrors the previous ayah exactly, but the conditional إن أمسك رزقه adds a terrifying specificity: if He withholds. Not "if He punishes," but simply: if He stops giving. The answer to the question (فمن يرزقكم) is elided, understood from context (Zuhayli), and the silence where the answer should be is itself the point. There is no one.

Ibn Ashur notes that this ayah is ناظر (looking back) to وكلوا من رزقه in ayah 15, following the principle of اللف والنشر المعكوس (inverse correlation). Ayah 20 (about نصر) corresponds to فامشوا في مناكبها (traversability, safety of the earth), while ayah 21 (about رزق) corresponds to وكلوا من رزقه (provision). The order is reversed, and the reversal links the two passages architecturally.

Maturidi reads the رزق concretely: the mushrikin depended on السماء (rain) and الأرض (vegetation). If God withheld the مطر or ceased to make the earth yield النبات, no one could substitute. And they knew this, because when القحط والجدوبة (drought and barrenness) struck, they turned to God in supplication, not to their idols.

Then comes the verdict: بل لجوا في عتو ونفور. The بل is for إضراب (Ibn Ashur): it is an استئناف بياني, answering the implicit question "did these proofs and warnings affect them at all?" The answer: no. They لجوا (persisted stubbornly in dispute), wrapped in عتو (arrogant defiance, the condition of المارد الشديد السفه, as Maturidi defines it) and نفور (recoiling from truth). Zamakhshari captures the combined force: تمادوا في عناد وشراد عن الحق لثقله عليهم, they continued in obstinacy and flight from the truth because it was heavy on them. The ظرفية (في عتو) is again مجازية (Ibn Ashur): عتو and نفور surround them so thoroughly that no escape route to الحق remains.

## Ayah 22

> أَفَمَنْ يَمْشِي مُكِبًّا عَلَى وَجْهِهِ أَهْدَى أَمَّنْ يَمْشِي سَوِيًّا عَلَى صِرَاطٍ مُسْتَقِيمٍ

Within the ابتلاء framework, this is the metaphor for pass versus fail. Two walkers. One stumbles face-down; the other walks upright on a straight road. Every mufassir agrees the first is the كافر and the second the مؤمن. But Ibn Ashur is the only one who asks the question that matters: why does the first image necessarily correspond to the mushrik rather than the believer? After all, each side claims to be on the straight path. Without a structural answer, the parable becomes a mere assertion that either side could dismiss.

His answer is the strongest reading of this ayah. It lies in the number of paths. The mushrik worships multiple scattered deities, unsure which one is stronger or more favorable to his tribe. Thaqif worshipped اللات. The Aws and Khazraj worshipped مناة. Each قبيلة had its own god or gods. The mushrik is like a traveler at a branching, unmarked junction who bends down to examine faint footprints (آثار أقدام الناس), trying to guess which trail is traveled and which abandoned. He is مكب on his face because the multiplicity of paths forces him to grope. The موحد, by contrast, worships One God with certainty. His road does not fork. He walks upright because there is nothing to search for on the ground, only a single direction to face. Ibn Ashur supports this with two cross-references: أأرباب متفرقون خير أم الله الواحد القهار (Yusuf 39), and وأن هذا صراطي مستقيما فاتبعوه ولا تتبعوا السبل فتفرق بكم عن سبيله (al-An'am 153), where the straight path (الإسلام) is explicitly contrasted with the multiple branching سبل (of شرك).

This reading means the ayah contains not one but three layered استعارات تمثيلية (Ibn Ashur). First: the mushrik's spiritual fragmentation is likened to a man walking on rough, unmarked terrain. Second (nested within the first): his bending down to examine tracks is likened to the مكب, the man whose face is pressed toward the ground. Third: the believer's certainty is likened to a man walking سويا on a جادة واضحة (a clear, well-trodden road). The omission of any صراط in the first half is itself meaningful: the مكب walks on no stated path, and the crooked character of his route is understood only from the contrast with مستقيم in the second half (Ibn Ashur: إيجاز حذف).

Linguistically, the word أهدى is مسلوب المفاضلة (stripped of real comparison), because the مكب has no هداية at all (Ibn Ashur). The تفضيل is rhetorical, like السجن أحب إلي (Yusuf 33), where prison is not genuinely "preferred" but simply less terrible than the alternative. There is an element of تهكم (sarcasm) or تمليح (ironic understatement): is the face-down stumbler really more guided?

Maturidi takes the ayah in a different direction that reinforces the ابتلاء theme. If both the ضال and the مهتدي pass through this world under the same outward conditions, and if each side accuses the other of error, then there must be a دار أخرى (another abode) for الجزاء where the truth of each claim is settled. The very existence of the مثل, with its two walkers reaching two different destinations, is itself a proof of البعث.

The parable also applies eschatologically. The Prophet (peace be upon him) was asked how people could be حشر on their faces, and he replied: أليس الذي أمشاهم على أرجلهم قادرا على أن يمشيهم على وجوههم (Bukhari and Muslim, via Ibn Kathir). The metaphor in this world becomes literal reality in the next: the believer will be resurrected walking upright on a مستقيم path to الجنة, while the disbeliever will be gathered on his face toward النار.

## Ayah 23

> قُلْ هُوَ الَّذِي أَنْشَأَكُمْ وَجَعَلَ لَكُمُ السَّمْعَ وَالْأَبْصَارَ وَالْأَفْئِدَةَ قَلِيلًا مَا تَشْكُرُونَ

This is the critical echo of the surah. Recall ayah 10: the failed test-takers, already in Hell, confess لو كنا نسمع أو نعقل ما كنا في أصحاب السعير, "if only we had listened or reasoned, we would not be among the inmates of the Blaze." Their confession names exactly two faculties: السمع (hearing) and العقل (reason). Now, in ayah 23, God reveals that He gave them precisely those instruments, and more: السمع والأبصار والأفئدة, hearing, sight, and hearts. The circle closes. The test-takers failed not because they lacked the tools, but because they refused to use what they had been given.

Within the ابتلاء framework, this ayah reveals the equipment provided for the test. Ayah 2 announced that life and death exist ليبلوكم أيكم أحسن عملا. Ayahs 3-5 presented the exam hall (the sky, the stars). Ayahs 15-18 described the test field (the earth, its provisions, the warnings of destruction). Ayahs 20-21 posed the test questions. Ayah 22 illustrated what passing and failing look like. Now ayah 23 discloses the instruments: hearing to receive the message, sight to observe the signs, and the فؤاد to reason toward the conclusion. The argument becomes devastating: you cannot plead ignorance, because you were equipped.

Maturidi provides the most layered explanation of the three faculties. السمع allows knowledge of what is غائب (absent, distant): you hear reports of what you cannot see. الأبصار allow knowledge of what is حاضر (present, visible): you observe the signs directly. الأفئدة enable apprehension of حقائق الأشياء ومبادئ الأمور ومآلها, the realities of things, their origins and their outcomes. Together, these three form the complete apparatus through which humans access all types of knowledge. Nothing is missing from the kit.

He then offers a remarkable meditation on the act of creation itself. God created the human being في أظلم مكان وأضيق موضع (in the darkest, narrowest place: the womb), where no human science or craft can reach. There, in that darkness, He fashioned the organs of perception. This proves simultaneously: (1) His قدرة surpasses all created power, (2) His علم encompasses hidden things as fully as manifest ones, (3) He is متعال عن الأشباه (transcendent above all analogies), since He acts simultaneously in countless wombs across creation without being present in any location, (4) He is المنعم (the Benefactor) who nourished and protected the embryo from harm, and (5) His حكمة is infinite, since no rational being creates something it knows will never serve its purpose. The argument from the womb proves, in a single stroke, the five things the surah has been establishing from its opening.

The closing قليلا ما تشكرون is a كناية (metonymy) for complete absence of gratitude, not a statement that gratitude exists in small quantity (Ibn Ashur, Qurtubi). The real شكر, as Zuhayli explains, is not verbal repetition but the صرف (directing) of each نعمة toward the purpose for which God created it: using السمع to hear the truth, البصر to see God's signs, and الفؤاد to reason toward God's oneness. When these faculties are used for worldly gain but not for worship, the نعمة has been received but the شكر has been withheld entirely.

The shift from God speaking directly (in the preceding ayahs) to commanding the Prophet to speak (قل) is itself significant. Ibn Ashur reads it as تفنن في البيان (rhetorical variation) and ترفيع لقدر النبي (elevation of the Prophet's station), making him a co-participant in the تذكير. It is as though the argument, having been conducted by the divine voice, is now handed to the human messenger to drive home.

## Ayah 24

> قُلْ هُوَ الَّذِي ذَرَأَكُمْ فِي الْأَرْضِ وَإِلَيْهِ تُحْشَرُونَ

The final ayah in this sequence completes the arc. Within the ابتلاء framework, this is the dispersal into the test field and the collection for results. God scattered you across the earth; to Him you will be gathered.

The verb ذرأ is more specific than أنشأ (which appeared in ayah 23). Ibn Ashur distinguishes them precisely: الإنشاء is creation from nothing, while الذرء is الإكثار من الموجود, multiplying from existing stock. Ayah 23 spoke of the original creation (أنشأكم); ayah 24 speaks of the subsequent proliferation and dispersion. Ibn Kathir expands: بثكم ونشركم في أقطار الأرض وأرجائها, مع اختلاف ألسنتكم في لغاتكم وألوانكم, He scattered and spread you across the regions and extremities of the earth, with the diversity of your languages and your colours. Maturidi adds that the ذرء from a single origin (من نفس واحدة) is itself a proof of قدرة على الإعادة: the One who multiplied countless souls from one soul can certainly reassemble what He scattered.

The closing وإليه تحشرون carries more than it appears to. Ibn Ashur finds a subtle كناية operating through two degrees of indirection: الحشر (gathering) implies البعث (resurrection), and البعث implies الموت (death). So the mention of الحشر is an indirect reminder of الموت, which they already know is inevitable. This mirrors the surah's opening theme: الذي خلق الموت والحياة (ayah 2). The surah began with death and life as the instruments of the test; it now closes this section with dispersal and gathering as the test's bookends. You were scattered into the field. You will be collected for the reckoning.

Maturidi notices the rhetorical strategy of joining a contested claim with an uncontested one. الحشر was denied by some كفار. But ذرأكم في الأرض is something all acknowledge: they can see human diversity spread across the earth with their own eyes. The proof of the uncontested (God scattered you) serves as evidence for the contested (God will gather you). The One who distributed can reassemble. Ibn Kathir captures this symmetry in a single sentence: يجمعكم كما فرقكم ويعيدكم كما بدأكم, He will gather you as He dispersed you and restore you as He originated you.
