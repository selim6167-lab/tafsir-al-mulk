# Comparative Tafsir: Surah Al-Mulk, Ayahs 12-18

**Sources:** Ibn Kathir, Qurtubi, Zuhayli, Zamakhshari, Baghawi, Ibn Ashur, Maturidi, Safwat al-Tafasir

---

## Ayah 12: إِنَّ الَّذِينَ يَخْشَوْنَ رَبَّهُمْ بِالْغَيْبِ لَهُمْ مَغْفِرَةٌ وَأَجْرٌ كَبِيرٌ

### Core Meaning

This ayah functions as a structural counterpart to the preceding depiction of hellfire, following the Quranic method of pairing رَهبة (fear/dread) with رَغبة (aspiration/hope) (Ibn Ashur). After describing the fate of the كفّار who are cast into the سعير, God turns to promise those who fear Him بِالغَيْب a twofold reward: مغفرة (forgiveness) for their sins and أجر كبير (a great recompense), understood unanimously as الجنّة (Qurtubi, Baghawi, Maturidi, Zuhayli, Safwat).

The meaning of يَخْشَوْنَ رَبَّهُمْ بِالْغَيْبِ carries two main interpretations across the tafasir. The first: they fear God's punishment while it remains unseen and not yet witnessed, meaning they believe in عذاب الآخرة without having seen it (Qurtubi, Baghawi, Maturidi). The second: they fear God when they are unseen by other people, performing obedience in secret and refraining from sin when no human eye observes them (Ibn Kathir, Zuhayli, Safwat). Ibn Kathir supports this with the hadith of the سبعة يظلّهم الله, citing the man who refused the beautiful woman's advances saying إِنِّي أَخَافُ اللَّه, and the man who gave charity so secretly that his left hand did not know what his right hand spent. Ibn Kathir also cites the hadith of Anas from the Musnad of al-Bazzar, in which the Companions expressed concern that their state changed upon leaving the Prophet's company; the Prophet reassured them that maintaining sincerity toward God in secret and in public is not نِفاق (hypocrisy).

Maturidi offers the most extended theological discussion, arguing that every true believer (except the Mu'tazila, in his view) possesses خشية because خشية inherently implies both رجاء (hope) and خوف (fear). He explains that believers vary in the intensity of their fear according to how often they recall their own غفلة (heedlessness) toward God's blessings; the more one remembers one's shortcomings, the greater one's خشية. He draws an analogy to the awareness of death: all people are certain of death, yet those who think of it more often live in greater vigilance.

Ibn Ashur notes that المغفرة was placed before الأجر الكبير as a deliberate rhetorical arrangement following the principle of تقديم التخلية على التحلية (removing harm before granting benefit). Believers first worry about past sins (including كفر before Islam, or لمم), so the promise of forgiveness is more urgently reassuring, after which the promise of reward follows. The تنكير (indefinite form) of مغفرة serves تعظيم (magnification), reinforced by its pairing with أجر كبير.

### Linguistic Analysis

- **يَخْشَوْنَ** (root: خ ش ي): denotes a fear rooted in reverence and awe, distinct from mere خوف. Multiple tafasir treat it as combining both خوف (fear of punishment) and رجاء (hope for mercy) (Maturidi).
- **بِالغَيْبِ**: the بِ here is either ظرفيّة (locative, "in the state of being unseen/while the punishment is unseen") or سببيّة (causal). The double interpretation (unseen punishment vs. unseen by people) is noted by virtually all mufassirun.
- **مَغْفِرَةٌ وَأَجْرٌ كَبِيرٌ**: Ibn Ashur observes that تقديم المسند على المسند إليه (fronting the predicate لَهُمْ before the subject مغفرة) achieves three effects: enabling the تنكير of the مبتدأ, signaling الاهتمام (emphasis), and maintaining the فاصلة (end-rhyme).
- **Rhetorical device**: Ibn Ashur identifies the ayah as an اعتراض (parenthetical insertion) that also functions as an استئناف بياني (explanatory resumption), placed between the description of hellfire and the subsequent address to the كفّار.

### Points of Divergence

**Issue: The referent of بِالغَيْبِ**
- View 1 (Qurtubi, Baghawi, Maturidi): they fear God's punishment while it (the punishment/the Day of Judgment) is غائب (unseen, not yet witnessed).
- View 2 (Ibn Kathir, Zuhayli, Safwat): they fear God when they themselves are غائبون عن أعين الناس (hidden from people's sight), so they obey Him سرّاً وعلانية (in private and in public).
- Zuhayli presents both views side by side without preference.

**Issue: The scope of خشية among believers**
- View (Maturidi): every believer, by definition, possesses خشية, though they vary in degree. The Mu'tazila and Khawarij, in his analysis, cannot be said to truly fear God or hope in His mercy, because their theology makes salvation and damnation dependent entirely on the servant's own action, leaving no room for genuine رجاء in God's فضل.
- Other mufassirun do not enter this theological debate in their commentary on this ayah.

### Unique Insights

- **Maturidi** provides an extended refutation of the Mu'tazili position on خشية and رجاء. He argues that on the Mu'tazili account, a person who commits a كبيرة and then asks God for forgiveness is effectively asking God to commit injustice (جور), since, on their view, God is obligated to punish the sinner. Conversely, a person who avoids major sins and still fears God is, on the Mu'tazili account, fearing God's injustice, which contradicts true معرفة (knowledge of God). This makes the entire concept of دعاء on رغبة ورهبة incoherent within Mu'tazili theology.
- **Ibn Kathir** uniquely supports the ayah with a hadith from al-Bazzar about the Companions' concern over spiritual inconsistency between the Prophet's presence and their private lives.
- **Ibn Ashur** observes the principle of تقديم التخلية على التحلية (or تقديم دفع الضرّ على جلب النفع) as the reason forgiveness is mentioned before reward.

## Ayah 13: وَأَسِرُّوا قَوْلَكُمْ أَوِ اجْهَرُوا بِهِ إِنَّهُ عَلِيمٌ بِذَاتِ الصُّدُورِ

### Core Meaning

Despite its imperative form, the ayah conveys the meaning of تسوية (equalisation): whether you conceal your speech or declare it openly, God knows it all equally (Zamakhshari, Ibn Ashur, Qurtubi). The address is primarily directed at the مشركون who, upon learning that God was informing the Prophet of their secret plots, told one another: أَسِرُّوا قَوْلَكُمْ كَيْ لَا يَسْمَعَ إِلَهُ مُحَمَّدٍ ("keep your speech secret so that the god of Muhammad does not hear") (Ibn Abbas, cited by Baghawi, Qurtubi, Zuhayli, Safwat). God responded by affirming that He is عليم بذات الصدور, encompassing not merely what is spoken aloud or whispered, but the very خواطر (thoughts), نوايا (intentions), and تقادير (deliberations) that arise within the hearts before any word is uttered (Ibn Ashur, Zamakhshari, Maturidi).

Zuhayli notes that السرّ was mentioned before الجهر because speech begins as a thought in the نفس before being articulated, and because the emphasis is on warning against the false security of concealment.

### Linguistic Analysis

- **أَسِرُّوا... أَوِ اجْهَرُوا**: the imperative form here is مُسْتَعْمَلة في التسوية (used for equalisation), analogous to فَاصْبِرُوا أَوْ لَا تَصْبِرُوا [الطور: 16] (Ibn Ashur, Zamakhshari).
- **ذَاتِ الصُّدُورِ**: ذات is the feminine of ذو (possessor). Maturidi explains that الصدر is the "courtyard of the heart" (ساحة القلب), so named because الآراء تصدر عنها (opinions issue from it). Ibn Ashur defines ذات الصدور as the خواطر, تقادير, and نوايا that revolve in the self. Qurtubi compares it to calling a child in the womb ذا بطنها.
- **إِنَّهُ عَلِيمٌ**: the pronoun هُ returns to الله المعلوم من المقام (God, understood from context), even though there is no explicit prior noun for it to refer back to in the immediate clause (Ibn Ashur).
- **Balagha**: Zuhayli identifies طِباق (antithesis) between أَسِرُّوا and اجْهَرُوا. Also سجع (assonance) between كبير/الخبير/الصدور/النشور.

### Points of Divergence

**Issue: Scope of address**
- View 1 (Ibn Abbas, via Baghawi, Qurtubi): the ayah was revealed specifically about the مشركون plotting against the Prophet.
- View 2 (Zuhayli, Safwat): while the سبب النزول concerns the مشركون, the آية is a خطاب عام لجميع الخلق في جميع الأعمال (a general address to all creation regarding all deeds).

### Unique Insights

- **Zamakhshari** reports the backstory in which the مشركون literally said أَسِرُّوا قَوْلَكُمْ لِئَلَّا يَسْمَعَهُ إِلَهُ مُحَمَّدٍ, and God exposed the absurdity of their reasoning: He knows what has not even been formulated into words, let alone whispered.
- **Maturidi** grounds the ayah in the theology of الوعيد (threat): the purpose is to affirm that God knows the أفعال and أقوال that people conceal in their صدور, and this knowledge is the basis for holding them accountable.

## Ayah 14: أَلَا يَعْلَمُ مَنْ خَلَقَ وَهُوَ اللَّطِيفُ الْخَبِيرُ

### Core Meaning

This ayah functions as an استئناف بياني (explanatory resumption) answering an implicit question: how can God know what is in the صدور? The answer: because He is the one who created them (Ibn Ashur, Zamakhshari). The argument is: the خالق (Creator) necessarily knows His مخلوق (creation). The one who fashioned the hearts and their capacity to harbour secrets cannot be ignorant of what those hearts contain (Qurtubi, Ibn Kathir, Safwat). Qurtubi cites a striking anecdote from Ibn al-Musayyab: a man standing in a dense forest on a windy night, watching leaves fall by the thousands, wondered in his heart whether God could know the trajectory of every leaf. A voice called out from the غيضة (thicket): أَلَا يَعْلَمُ مَنْ خَلَقَ وَهُوَ اللَّطِيفُ الْخَبِيرُ.

The ayah concludes with two divine names. اللطيف: the One whose knowledge penetrates the finest details and hidden matters (Zuhayli, Maturidi). الخبير: the One who knows all things, both ظاهر (manifest) and باطن (hidden), including what has not yet occurred (Qurtubi, citing Abu Ishaq al-Isfarayini's taxonomy of divine names relating to knowledge).

### Linguistic Analysis

- **مَنْ خَلَقَ**: two i'rab possibilities are discussed extensively:
  - (a) مَنْ is the فاعل (subject) of يَعْلَمُ, referring to God: "Does not the Creator know?" The مفعول (object) is implied from context (i.e., the secrets). This is preferred by Ibn Kathir, Zamakhshari, and Zuhayli.
  - (b) مَنْ is the مفعول (object) of يَعْلَمُ, referring to the created beings: "Does not God know those whom He created?" This reading implies an omitted subject (الله).
  - Ibn Kathir states the first reading (مَنْ = the Creator) is أولى because of the subsequent وَهُوَ اللَّطِيفُ الْخَبِيرُ, which describes the subject's quality. Zamakhshari provides a detailed logical argument: the حال clause (وَهُوَ اللَّطِيفُ الْخَبِيرُ) constrains the reading, because saying "does He not know, while He is all-knowing" would be tautological, but "does He not know this, while He is all-knowing of everything" is sound.
- **اللَّطِيفُ**: Maturidi explains it as God's subtle تدبير in designing the tongue for speech and the heart for conceiving and storing secrets, in ways that the creation itself cannot fathom. Zuhayli defines it as العالم بدقائق الأمور وخفاياها التي لا يدركها العالمون.
- **الخَبِيرُ**: Qurtubi, citing Abu Ishaq al-Isfarayini, provides a taxonomy of divine names of knowledge: العليم (all-encompassing knowledge), الخبير (knowledge of what will be before it is), الحكيم (knowledge of the finest qualities), الشهيد (knowledge of the absent and present), الحافظ (the One who does not forget), المحصي (the One for whom multiplicity is no obstacle to knowledge).

### Points of Divergence

**Issue: The i'rab of مَنْ خَلَقَ**
- View 1 (Ibn Kathir, Zamakhshari, Zuhayli, Safwat): مَنْ is the فاعل of يَعْلَمُ, meaning "the Creator." Preferred as the stronger reading.
- View 2 (some scholars, including Ja'far ibn Harb and Abu Bakr al-Asamm, cited by Maturidi): مَنْ is the مفعول, meaning "those whom He created." Maturidi refutes this reading at length, arguing it would strip the ayah of its وعيد function, since merely knowing that creatures exist (without knowing their deeds) does not ground accountability.
- Ibn Ashur accepts both as linguistically sound but notes the theological implication: the first reading directly proves God's عموم العلم (comprehensiveness of knowledge), while the second is less direct.

**Issue: Theological implication for خلق أفعال العباد (God's creation of human acts)**
- View (Maturidi): the ayah constitutes حجّة (proof) for the Sunni position that God creates the أفعال and أقوال of His servants, because the argument "does not the Creator know what He created" presupposes that the سرّ and الجهر are themselves مخلوقات (created). The Mu'tazili counter-reading (مَنْ = the created being) is intended to avoid this implication.
- View (Ibn Ashur): the ayah is a proof of عموم العلم (the universality of divine knowledge), but it does not directly prove خلق أفعال العباد, because there is an انفكاك ظاهر (clear disjunction) between the تعلّق of العلم and the تعلّق of القدرة.
- View (Zamakhshari, fn.): in the footnotes of Ahmad ibn al-Munir to the Kashshaf, this ayah is claimed by أهل السنة as proof that the servant does not create his own acts, using the argument: if God creates and therefore knows, then the servant who does not know (his own acts exhaustively) does not create.

### Unique Insights

- **Qurtubi's taxonomy** (via Abu Ishaq al-Isfarayini) of six divine names of knowledge (العليم, الخبير, الحكيم, الشهيد, الحافظ, المحصي) and the specific nuance each carries is a distinctive contribution not found in the other tafasir.
- **Maturidi** offers a philosophical meditation on the design of the tongue and heart: God fashioned the tongue so that speech emerges from it, yet no one can discover the precise mechanism by which this happens. Similarly, God designed the heart to form images (يُصوِّر) and store secrets, in a manner that all creation, even if united, could not reverse-engineer.

## Ayah 15: هُوَ الَّذِي جَعَلَ لَكُمُ الْأَرْضَ ذَلُولًا فَامْشُوا فِي مَنَاكِبِهَا وَكُلُوا مِنْ رِزْقِهِ وَإِلَيْهِ النُّشُورُ

### Core Meaning

Having established God's knowledge and creative power, the discourse moves to His نعمة (beneficence) in making the earth تسخير (subservient) to humanity (Zuhayli, Ibn Ashur). The earth is described as ذلول, a term normally applied to a tamed, docile riding animal (Ibn Ashur, Qurtubi, Zamakhshari). The metaphor is striking: God has rendered the massive, solid earth as manageable for humans as a broken-in she-camel (Ibn Ashur). He then commands them to walk in her مناكب (shoulders/flanks) and eat من رزقه, before reminding them that the نشور (resurrection) is to Him alone.

Ibn Kathir explains that God made the earth قارّة ساكنة (stable and still), neither rocking nor shaking, by virtue of the mountains, springs, roads, and cultivable areas He placed in it. The imperative فَامْشُوا is an أمر إباحة (permission) showing الامتنان (grace), not an obligation (Qurtubi).

Maturidi offers a distinctive teleological argument: because God made the earth ذلولاً for humans, it cannot have been created عبثاً (in vain). There must be a return (نشور) to account for how the gift was used, just as a master who gives a servant resources will eventually demand an accounting.

### Linguistic Analysis

- **ذَلُولًا** (root: ذ ل ل): فَعُول بمعنى فاعل, meaning منقاد مطاوع (compliant, yielding). It applies equally to masculine and feminine (Ibn Ashur). The استعارة (metaphor) compares the earth to a riding beast that has been ارتياض (broken in), after having been صعبة (unruly). This is an استعارة تصريحية (explicit metaphor) (Ibn Ashur).
- **مَنَاكِبِهَا** (root: ن ك ب): the مَنْكِب is the junction of the shoulder and upper arm. Multiple interpretations exist:
  - جبالها (mountains): Ibn Abbas, Qatada, Abu al-Darda', Baghawi.
  - أطرافها ونواحيها (edges and regions): Mujahid, al-Suddi, al-Kalbi, Ibn Kathir.
  - طرقها وفجاجها (paths and passes): Mujahid (alternate), al-Hasan.
  - جوانبها (flanks): al-Farra', Qurtubi.
- **Balagha (Zamakhshari)**: the mention of مناكب is a مَثَل (parable/image) to convey فرط التذليل (the utmost degree of subjugation). On a riding camel, the منكبان and their junction at the غارب (withers) are the most delicate, elevated point, the place a rider would never dare step on. If God declares the earth so subdued that you can walk even on its مناكب, then no part of it has been left unmastered. This is the most refined explanation of the image.
- **Zamakhshari also notes** a secondary reading (attributed to al-Zajjaj): if you can walk on the جبال (the hardest, most inaccessible terrain), then the rest of the earth is a fortiori even more traversable.
- **وَإِلَيْهِ النُّشُورُ**: تقديم المجرور (fronting the prepositional phrase) serves الاهتمام (emphasis) and حصر (restriction): the resurrection is to Him alone, not to anyone else (Ibn Ashur). The mention of نشور after the earth is thematically apt because the بعث will be من الأرض (from the earth) (Ibn Ashur).

### Points of Divergence

**Issue: The meaning of مَنَاكِبِهَا**
- View 1 (Ibn Abbas, Qatada, Abu al-Darda'): الجبال (mountains).
- View 2 (Mujahid, al-Suddi, al-Hasan, al-Kalbi, al-Farra'): أطرافها / جوانبها / طرقها (edges, flanks, paths).
- Zamakhshari and Ibn Ashur treat مناكب as a تخييل (imaginative embellishment) completing the ذلول metaphor, rather than a literal geographic designation.

**Issue: The function of the imperative فَامْشُوا**
- View 1 (Qurtubi): أمر إباحة (permissive command), showing الامتنان.
- View 2 (Ibn Ashur): أمر مستعمل في الإدامة (command of continuation), a reminder of what God has already enabled, functioning as امتنان.
- View 3 (Maturidi): the command invites them to travel the earth and observe its flawless design, connecting to the earlier instruction فَارْجِعِ الْبَصَرَ هَلْ تَرَى مِنْ فُطُورٍ.

### Unique Insights

- **Ibn Ashur** develops the ذلول metaphor fully as an استعارة تصريحية, identifying المشي في مناكبها as a تخييل completing the image, and وكلوا من رزقه as a further extension: just as one benefits from a riding animal by riding it and consuming its milk and meat, one benefits from the earth by walking on it and eating its produce.
- **Maturidi** reads the ayah as part of the argument for البعث (resurrection): if God did not create the earth عبثاً, there must be a return for accountability.
- **Ibn Kathir** cites the hadith of عمر بن الخطاب: لَوْ أَنَّكُمْ تَتَوَكَّلُونَ عَلَى اللَّهِ حَقَّ تَوَكُّلِهِ لَرَزَقَكُمْ كَمَا يَرْزُقُ الطَّيْرَ تَغْدُو خِمَاصًا وَتَرُوحُ بِطَانًا, affirming that السعي في السبب لا ينافي التوكل (seeking means does not contradict trust in God). Safwat and Zuhayli both cite the report of عمر passing a group who called themselves المتوكلون, to which he replied: بل أنتم المتواكلون (you are the idle dependents), insisting that the true متوكّل is one who plants his seed and then trusts God.

## Ayah 16: أَأَمِنْتُمْ مَنْ فِي السَّمَاءِ أَنْ يَخْسِفَ بِكُمُ الْأَرْضَ فَإِذَا هِيَ تَمُورُ

### Core Meaning

This ayah marks an transition from استدلال (argumentation) to تخويف (intimidation) (Ibn Ashur). Having established that God created the earth as ذلول for humanity, the discourse pivots: the same God who tamed the earth can also unmake it beneath your feet. Have you grown so secure that you feel safe from the One whose ملكوت (dominion) is in the السماء, that He might cause the earth to خسف (swallow/cave in), leaving it تمور (heaving and convulsing)? (Ibn Kathir, Zuhayli, Safwat).

Ibn Kathir frames this as a display of God's لطف ورحمة: He is قادر على تعذيبهم (capable of punishing them) due to their كفر, yet He يحلم ويصفح ويؤجّل ولا يعجّل (forbears, pardons, delays, and does not hasten). Maturidi reads the ayah as part of the محاجّة (argumentation) against the منكري البعث (deniers of resurrection): if you deny the آخرة, what makes you safe from عذاب الدنيا?

### Linguistic Analysis

- **أَأَمِنْتُمْ**: the استفهام is for إنكار وتوبيخ وتحذير (denial, rebuke, and warning) (Ibn Ashur). Maturidi adds the nuance that it can also be taken as تقريري (rhetorical assertion): قَدْ أَمِنْتُمْ ("you have indeed grown complacent").
- **مَنْ فِي السَّمَاءِ**: a significant theological term. Zamakhshari gives two readings: (a) the One whose ملكوت is in the السماء, since it is the abode of His ملائكة, عرش, كرسي, and لوح محفوظ, and from it descend His قضايا and أوامر; (b) addressing the مشركون on their own terms, since they held a تشبيهي (anthropomorphic) belief that God is physically in the sky, so the ayah responds: "even on your own assumption, do you feel safe from Him?" Qurtubi reports multiple interpretations: it may refer to God's عذاب (punishment), His قدرة (power), His سلطان (authority), the ملائكة, or specifically جبريل as الملك الموكّل بالعذاب. Qurtubi also affirms the Sunni theological position: the mention of السماء points to God's علوّ (transcendence) and عظمة, not to مكان (spatial location) or جهة (direction), since He is منزّه عن الأمكنة والجهات والحدود. Maturidi interprets it as: God is إله السماء (the God of the heaven), which does not negate that He is also إله الأرض.
- **يَخْسِفَ**: the خسف is the inversion of the earth's surface, its ظاهر becoming باطن and vice versa (Ibn Ashur). A severe form of زلزال (earthquake). The باء in بكم is for المصاحبة (accompaniment): the earth caves in along with you (Ibn Ashur). Zuhayli notes that أَنْ يَخْسِفَ is in the position of بدل اشتمال from مَنْ فِي السماء.
- **تَمُورُ** (root: م و ر): to move back and forth, to heave and convulse (Qurtubi, Baghawi). Qurtubi explains: when the earth swallows someone, it rotates (دارت) around them, and that circular motion is المور. Ibn Ashur uses the term الارتجاج والاضطراب.
- **فَإِذَا هِيَ تَمُورُ**: إِذا الفجائيّة (the particle of surprise). Ibn Ashur provides an extended analysis: this particle normally denotes something happening in the present, but here it is applied to a future/hypothetical event to make the threat feel as vivid and immediate as if it had already occurred, a technique he calls تمثيليّة مكنيّة (implicit similitude). This is analogous to the Quranic usage in ثُمَّ إِذَا دَعَاكُمْ دَعْوَةً مِنَ الْأَرْضِ إِذَا أَنْتُمْ تَخْرُجُونَ [الروم: 25].

### Points of Divergence

**Issue: The identity of مَنْ فِي السَّمَاءِ**
- View 1 (Zamakhshari, option a; majority): the One whose ملكوت and سلطان are in السماء. This avoids any implication of مكان (spatial location) for God.
- View 2 (Zamakhshari, option b): responding to the مشركون on their own تشبيهي assumptions.
- View 3 (Qurtubi, some): the reference is to the ملائكة or specifically to جبريل as the angel of destruction. Ibn Ashur accepts this as linguistically viable, since مَنْ can refer to angels, and the verb يَخْسِفَ can be truly ascribed to them as فاعلون of the act by God's command.
- View 4 (Maturidi): مَنْ فِي السَّمَاءِ means God, described as being in the السماء with respect to His ملك وسلطان, not His ذات.

### Unique Insights

- **Maturidi** offers three distinct angles for the ayah's argument: (1) against the deniers of البعث: since you know the difference between عدوّ and وليّ, how can you feel safe from punishment in this world if you deny the next? (2) you acknowledge that God sends rain from the sky and sustenance from the earth, so why not also fear that He can send destruction from either direction? (3) you know what happened to previous nations who were destroyed by خسف or by stones from the sky, so how can you be complacent while repeating their تكذيب?
- **Ibn Ashur** identifies the juxtaposition of السماء and الأرض as a محسّن طباق (rhetorical antithesis).

## Ayah 17: أَمْ أَمِنْتُمْ مَنْ فِي السَّمَاءِ أَنْ يُرْسِلَ عَلَيْكُمْ حَاصِبًا فَسَتَعْلَمُونَ كَيْفَ نَذِيرِ

### Core Meaning

The threat escalates. After the خسف from below, God now warns of the حاصب from above: a wind carrying حصباء (stoning pebbles/gravel) that will crush them, as He did to قوم لوط and أصحاب الفيل (Qurtubi, Zuhayli, Baghawi, Safwat). The ayah concludes with a chilling warning: فَسَتَعْلَمُونَ كَيْفَ نَذِيرِ, "you will come to know how My warning [was]," meaning that when the punishment arrives, they will finally understand the truth of the إنذار, but this knowledge will come too late to benefit them (Zamakhshari, Zuhayli).

Ibn Ashur explains the transition from ayah 16 to 17 via the particle أَمْ, which here serves الإضراب الانتقالي (transitional shift), moving from one type of إنكار to another. The first concerned a terrestrial punishment (خسف), and the second concerns a celestial one (حاصب), which is أمكن (more within reach) for مَنْ فِي السَّمَاءِ and أشدّ وقعاً (more devastating in impact) on أهل الأرض. He also notes that the خسف was mentioned first because it relates to الأرض (which was the topic of the preceding ayah), and the حاصب second because it relates to السماء, following a pattern of نشر معكوس (inverted distribution).

### Linguistic Analysis

- **حَاصِبًا** (root: ح ص ب): Qurtubi gives three readings: (a) حجارة من السماء (stones from the sky), as sent on قوم لوط and أصحاب الفيل; (b) ريح فيها حجارة وحصباء (a wind carrying stones and gravel); (c) سحاب فيه حجارة (a cloud bearing stones). Ibn Kathir and Zuhayli prefer the sense of ريح فيها حصباء. Baghawi specifies ريح ذات حجارة.
- **نَذِيرِ**: a مصدر بمعنى الإنذار (verbal noun meaning "warning/the act of warning") (Ibn Ashur, Zamakhshari). Alternatively, it can be a وصف بمعنى المنذر (adjective meaning "the warner"), referring to the Prophet (Qurtubi). The ياء of the متكلّم (نذيري) is deleted for تخفيف and to maintain the فاصلة (Safwat, Ibn Ashur).
- **كَيْفَ نَذِيرِ**: the استفهام serves as a معلِّق (suspending particle) for the verb تَعْلَمُونَ, which takes an indirect question as its object (Ibn Ashur). The rhetorical force is تهديد وتهويل (threat and intimidation).
- **Balagha (Zuhayli)**: the sequence نَذِيرِ / نَكِيرِ / بَصِيرٌ constitutes سجع مرصّع (ornamental rhyming prose), a مراعاة لرءوس الآيات (conformity with verse-endings).

### Points of Divergence

**Issue: The meaning of نَذِيرِ**
- View 1 (Zamakhshari, Ibn Ashur): مصدر بمعنى الإنذار (the act of warning, i.e., God's warning).
- View 2 (Qurtubi): may also mean المُنْذِر (the warner), i.e., Muhammad, and the meaning becomes: you will know the truth of his mission.

### Unique Insights

- **Ibn Ashur** explains the sequencing of خسف before حاصب: first, the خسف is from الأرض, continuing the theme of the preceding ayah about the earth; second, the حاصب from the sky is أمكن (more naturally within reach) for مَنْ فِي السَّمَاءِ, and thus more terrifying as a second escalation. He also notes that the حاصب may correspond to how الأرزاق الأرضيّة originate from غيوث السماء (rains of the sky), per وَفِي السَّمَاءِ رِزْقُكُمْ [الذاريات: 22], so the source of sustenance can equally become the source of destruction.
- **Maturidi** reads the ayah as: you will come to know that the نُذُر (warners/prophets) who warned you were محقّون (truthful), and that their warnings were not lies as you claimed.

## Ayah 18: وَلَقَدْ كَذَّبَ الَّذِينَ مِنْ قَبْلِهِمْ فَكَيْفَ كَانَ نَكِيرِ

### Core Meaning

God shifts from direct address (خطاب) to third-person narration (إخبار بالغَيبة) about the مشركون, a rhetorical move signifying غضب (anger) and إبعاد (distancing), as if they no longer deserve the honor of being spoken to directly (Ibn Ashur). The message is historical and admonitory: past nations (قوم نوح, عاد, ثمود, قوم لوط, أصحاب مدين, أصحاب الرسّ, قوم فرعون) denied their رسل and suffered God's نكير (punitive response), which was عظيماً شديداً أليماً (tremendous, severe, and grievous) (Ibn Kathir, Qurtubi). The rhetorical question فَكَيْفَ كَانَ نَكِيرِ is a تقرير (confirmatory question) and كناية (metonymy) for the certainty and severity of God's retaliation (Ibn Ashur).

The ayah serves a dual function: تسلية للرسول (consolation for the Prophet) and تهديد لقومه المشركين (a threat to his people) (Zuhayli, Safwat). Maturidi states the purpose is ارتداع (deterrence): by reminding them of the fate of previous deniers, God seeks to prevent them from repeating the same تكذيب.

### Linguistic Analysis

- **وَلَقَدْ**: the لام and قد together serve تأكيد (emphatic assertion). Ibn Ashur explains the تنزيل (positioning): the مشركون are treated as though they might believe the previous nations were punished for reasons other than تكذيب, so the emphatic assertion corrects this misapprehension.
- **نَكِيرِ**: مصدر بمعنى الإنكار (Ibn Ashur, Zamakhshari), i.e., God's act of denial/repudiation through punishment. Like نَذِيرِ, the ياء of the متكلّم (نكيري) is dropped. Qurtubi notes the qira'at: Warsh retains the ياء in وصل (continuous reading), Ya'qub retains it in both وصل and وقف, and the rest omit it following the مصحف.
- **الالتفات** (rhetorical shift): Ibn Ashur identifies the shift from second person (أَأَمِنْتُمْ) to third person (الَّذِينَ مِنْ قَبْلِهِمْ) as an الْتِفات motivated by غضب. He notes that God did not say وَلَقَدْ كَذَّبَ الَّذِينَ مِنْ قَبْلِكُمْ (with second-person pronoun), which would have maintained the direct address.
- **فَكَيْفَ كَانَ نَكِيرِ**: Ibn Ashur identifies this as a combination of استفهام تقريري (confirmatory question) and كناية (metonymy) for the فظاعة (severity) of the punishment. The implied answer is: it was devastating beyond description.

### Points of Divergence

**Issue: The syntactic relationship to the preceding ayah**
- View 1 (Ibn Ashur, primary): the واو is عاطفة (connective), linking this ayah to the threat in ayah 17, on the grounds that some of the previous nations were destroyed by خسف or حاصب (like قوم لوط).
- View 2 (Ibn Ashur, alternate): the واو is حاليّة (circumstantial), and the meaning becomes: "how can you feel safe while knowing that those before you denied and were destroyed?"

### Unique Insights

- **Ibn Ashur's** observation about the الْتِفات (shift from second to third person) as a sign of divine غضب is a distinctive rhetorical insight. He explains that by distancing the مشركون from direct address, God simultaneously continues to warn them (since they hear the third-person account) while signaling that their persistent تكذيب has cost them the عزّ الحضور (honor of being addressed directly).
- **Maturidi** frames the ayah as an argument from historical precedent: God reminds them of what befell previous deniers so that they might ارتدع (desist). He adds that the question فَكَيْفَ كَانَ نَكِيرِ demands the implicit answer: أَلَيْسَ وَجَدُوهُ شَدِيدًا وَحَقًّا ("did they not find it severe and true?!").
- **Qurtubi** lists specific nations: قوم نوح, عاد, ثمود, قوم لوط, أصحاب مدين, أصحاب الرسّ, and قوم فرعون, providing concrete historical referents for the general statement.

## Cross-Cutting Themes (Ayahs 12-18)

### The Pairing of رَهبة and رَغبة
The section opens with a promise to the خاشعون (ayah 12) immediately after the description of hellfire (ayahs 6-11), then transitions to warnings directed at the كفّار (ayahs 13-18). This alternation is a deliberate Quranic structural method identified explicitly by Ibn Ashur.

### God's Knowledge as the Foundation of Accountability
Ayahs 13-14 establish that God's علم extends to ذات الصدور, which is then leveraged in the threat sequence (ayahs 16-18). The logical chain: God knows your secrets (13), because He is the Creator (14), He blessed you with the earth (15), and He can equally destroy it beneath you or rain destruction upon you from above (16-17), as He did to previous nations (18).

### The Earth as Gift and Threat
Ayah 15 presents the earth as God's gift (ذلول), while ayahs 16-17 present the very same earth (and sky) as instruments of punishment. This ironic reversal is noted by Zuhayli, who summarizes: the God who ذلّلها for you can also خسفها بكم.

### The Zamakhshari Distinction on صافّات / يقبضن (Ayah 19 preview)
Although ayah 19 falls beyond our range, the tafasir of ayahs 16-18 set up the argument it completes. After threatening with خسف and حاصب, God points to the birds as براهين (proofs) of His قدرة. Zamakhshari's observation that صافّات is expressed as an اسم فاعل (for the default, sustained state of wing-spread) while يقبضن is a فعل مضارع (for the occasional, non-default act of wing-folding) was widely adopted by later mufassirun (Zuhayli, Safwat).
