# Surah Al-Mulk (67): Ayahs 12-18

## Ayah 12

> إِنَّ الَّذِينَ يَخْشَوْنَ رَبَّهُمْ بِالْغَيْبِ لَهُمْ مَغْفِرَةٌ وَأَجْرٌ كَبِيرٌ

The surah has just finished its portrait of the people of the Fire: they heard the warning, rejected it, and confessed too late. Now, in a single ayah, it pivots to the people who pass the test. The placement is deliberate. The Quran consistently pairs رهبة (dread) with رغبة (aspiration), and Ibn Ashur identifies this ayah as an اعتراض (parenthetical) that doubles as an استئناف بياني: it answers an unspoken question from the believers who have been listening to the preceding horror. Where do we stand?

The answer hinges on two words: يخشون and بالغيب. Two readings of بالغيب run through the tradition, and both illuminate the ayah. The first: they fear God's punishment while it is still unseen, while the Day of Judgement remains غائب. This is the reading of faith as conviction without evidence, the opposite of the people of the Fire who only acknowledged the truth once they saw it. The believer fears what he has not witnessed; the disbeliever feared nothing until it was too late (Maturidi, Qurtubi, Baghawi). The second: they fear God when they themselves are unseen by people, when no human eye is watching. Ibn Kathir supports this with the hadith of the seven whom God will shade: among them, the man who refused the beautiful woman saying إني أخاف الله, and the man whose left hand did not know what his right hand gave. Both readings describe the same quality from different angles. To fear an unseen punishment is to take the test seriously; to fear God when unseen by others is to know that the Examiner is always watching.

The promise is twofold: مغفرة وأجر كبير. Ibn Ashur notices that forgiveness comes first, following the principle of تقديم التخلية على التحلية (removing harm before granting benefit). The believer's first anxiety is about past sins, the كفر before Islam, the لمم, the accumulated shortfalls. The Quran addresses that fear before the promise of reward. The indefinite form مغفرة serves تعظيم: a forgiveness so great it cannot be bounded by the definite article.

Maturidi takes the theology of خشية deeper than anyone else. Every true believer, he argues, possesses خشية, but believers vary in its intensity according to how often they recall their own غفلة toward God's blessings. His analogy is death: all people are certain of death, yet those who think of it more often live in greater vigilance. The one who remembers his heedlessness more is the one who fears more. This is not a binary quality but a spectrum, and the ayah is addressing those at the upper end of it.

## Ayahs 13-14

> وَأَسِرُّوا قَوْلَكُمْ أَوِ اجْهَرُوا بِهِ إِنَّهُ عَلِيمٌ بِذَاتِ الصُّدُورِ
>
> أَلَا يَعْلَمُ مَنْ خَلَقَ وَهُوَ اللَّطِيفُ الْخَبِيرُ

If ayah 12 describes the student who takes the test seriously, ayahs 13-14 describe the Examiner. You cannot cheat on this test.

The backstory is almost comic. The مشركون, learning that God was informing the Prophet of their secret conversations, told each other: أسروا قولكم كي لا يسمع إله محمد, "whisper so that Muhammad's god doesn't hear" (Ibn Abbas, via Baghawi, Qurtubi, Zamakhshari). God's response dismantles the absurdity in stages. The imperative form أسروا... أو اجهروا is not a command but a تسوية (equalisation): it makes no difference which you choose (Zamakhshari, Ibn Ashur). The analogy is فاصبروا أو لا تصبروا, "be patient or don't be patient," where the imperative strips the distinction of all meaning. Then the reason: إنه عليم بذات الصدور. He knows not merely what you whisper or declare, but what has not yet been formulated into words, the خواطر and نوايا that revolve in the self before the tongue moves (Ibn Ashur). The صدر, Maturidi explains, is the "courtyard of the heart" (ساحة القلب), named so because opinions issue from it (الآراء تصدر عنها). God knows the courtyard before anyone opens the door.

Then comes ayah 14, the knockout argument: ألا يعلم من خلق. Two grammatical readings of مَنْ خَلَقَ exist, and both genuinely deepen the ayah. If مَنْ is the subject (the Creator), the meaning is: "Does not the One who created know?" The argument is self-evident. The maker of a thing necessarily knows its workings. If مَنْ is the object (the created), the meaning is: "Does not God know what He created?" The emphasis shifts from the act of creating to the intimacy of the relationship between maker and made.

Ibn Kathir and Zamakhshari prefer the first reading, and Zamakhshari provides a precise reason. The حال clause وهو اللطيف الخبير constrains the grammar: saying "does He not know, while He is all-knowing" would be tautological, but saying "does He not know this, while He is all-knowing of everything" is sound. The clause demands a specific object of knowledge (the secrets), not knowledge in the abstract. Maturidi, defending the same reading, adds a theological point: making مَنْ the object would reduce the ayah to "God knows His creatures exist," which strips it of its وعيد (threat) function. The ayah is not asserting that God knows creatures are there; it is asserting that the Creator knows every secret those creatures harbour. Both readings, however, converge on the same truth. The one who made you knows you from the inside.

The closing names are precise. اللطيف is not gentleness here but penetration: God's knowledge reaches the finest details, the دقائق that no other knower can access (Zuhayli). Maturidi gives this a striking illustration. God fashioned the tongue so that speech emerges from it, yet no one can discover the precise mechanism by which this happens. He designed the heart to form images and store secrets in a manner that all creation, even if united, could not reverse-engineer. The designer of these systems knows their outputs before they appear. الخبير is the knower of what is hidden, the one whose knowledge extends to what has not yet occurred and what will never be reported to anyone (Qurtubi, via Abu Ishaq al-Isfarayini).

## Ayah 15

> هُوَ الَّذِي جَعَلَ لَكُمُ الْأَرْضَ ذَلُولًا فَامْشُوا فِي مَنَاكِبِهَا وَكُلُوا مِنْ رِزْقِهِ وَإِلَيْهِ النُّشُورُ

The test has an Examiner who sees everything (ayahs 13-14). Now: the test has a venue. The earth is the exam hall, and God is the one who built it for you.

The word ذلول is the key image. It is a term for a broken-in riding animal, a she-camel that has been ارتياض (trained) after having been صعبة (unruly) (Ibn Ashur). The earth, for all its mass and solidity, has been rendered as manageable as a docile mount. This is an استعارة تصريحية (explicit metaphor), and Zamakhshari draws out its full force through the word مناكب. The منكب is the junction of the shoulder and upper arm, the most delicate and elevated point on a camel's body, the place a rider would never dare step. On a real camel, you avoid the مناكب because the animal will buck. If God declares this earth so subdued that you can walk even on its مناكب, then no part of it is beyond your reach. That is Zamakhshari's reading: فرط التذليل, the uttermost degree of subjugation.

Others took مناكب more literally. Ibn Abbas and Qatadah said it means الجبال (mountains); Mujahid said طرقها وفجاجها (paths and passes); al-Farra' said جوانبها (flanks). Zamakhshari reports a secondary reading from al-Zajjaj that synthesises these: if you can walk on the جبال (the hardest, most inaccessible terrain), then the rest of the earth is a fortiori even easier. But the metaphorical reading carries more force. Ibn Ashur completes the image: just as one benefits from a riding animal by riding it and consuming its milk and meat, one benefits from the earth by walking on it and eating its produce. Walking is the riding; eating is the milking. The metaphor is seamless.

The command فامشوا is an أمر إباحة (Qurtubi), not an obligation but a reminder of what God has already enabled. Ibn Ashur calls it an أمر مستعمل في الإدامة: a command of continuation, pointing to a gift already in operation. And Ibn Kathir adds the practical corollary: فالسعي في السبب لا ينافي التوكل, seeking means does not contradict trust in God. The hadith of the birds illustrates this: "If you relied on God with true reliance, He would provide for you as He provides for the birds: they go out hungry in the morning and return full in the evening." The birds go out. They do not sit.

Maturidi reads the whole ayah through the lens of ابتلاء (testing). If God made the earth ذلولا for humans, it was not created عبثا (in vain). There must be a reckoning for how the gift was used. Just as a master who gives a servant resources will eventually demand an accounting, the one who tamed the earth for you will ask what you did with it. This is why the ayah ends with وإليه النشور. The نشور (resurrection) follows the نعمة (provision) as its logical consequence. Ibn Ashur adds that the mention of نشور after the earth is thematically apt because the بعث itself will be من الأرض, from the earth. You walk on it, eat from it, are buried in it, and rise from it. The exam hall is also the departure lounge.

## Ayahs 16-17

> أَأَمِنْتُمْ مَنْ فِي السَّمَاءِ أَنْ يَخْسِفَ بِكُمُ الْأَرْضَ فَإِذَا هِيَ تَمُورُ
>
> أَمْ أَمِنْتُمْ مَنْ فِي السَّمَاءِ أَنْ يُرْسِلَ عَلَيْكُمْ حَاصِبًا فَسَتَعْلَمُونَ كَيْفَ نَذِيرِ

The tone shifts violently. Ayah 15 described the earth as a gift, ذلولا, tame and generous. Now the same earth becomes a weapon. The God who tamed it for you can collapse it beneath your feet. This is the surah's most unsettling move: the exam hall itself can be destroyed at any moment.

Ibn Ashur identifies the transition as a shift from استدلال (argumentation) to تخويف (intimidation). The logic is tight. It was established that God created the earth and made it ذلولا for you (ayah 15). You did not honour the one who did this. Therefore, He can reverse it. Your مشي في مناكبها can become تجلجل في طبقاتها, your strolling on its shoulders can become plunging through its layers.

The threat comes in two movements, from below and from above, a pincer. Ayah 16: the earth opens and swallows, then heaves (تمور). Ayah 17: stones rain from the sky (حاصب), a wind carrying حصباء that crushes what it strikes (Ibn Kathir, Zuhayli). Ibn Ashur notices the sequencing. The خسف (from below) comes first because ayah 15 was about the earth, and the حاصب (from above) comes second because it originates from the sky. This follows a pattern of نشر معكوس (inverted distribution). He adds a further irony: the أرزاق الأرضية (earthly provisions) originate from غيوث السماء (rains of heaven), as God says وفي السماء رزقكم (al-Dhariyat: 22). The same source that feeds you can destroy you.

The particle إذا in فإذا هي تمور is إذا الفجائية, the particle of surprise. Ibn Ashur notes that it normally denotes something happening in the present, but here it is applied to a future threat to make it feel as vivid and immediate as if it had already occurred. The technique is تمثيلية مكنية: the hypothetical is presented as accomplished fact. You are not merely warned that the earth might heave; you are made to feel it heaving.

Maturidi reads these ayahs as three interlocking arguments against the deniers of resurrection. First: you know the difference between an enemy and a friend, so how can you feel safe from punishment in this world while you deny the next? Second: you acknowledge that God sends rain from the sky and provision from the earth, so why not also fear that He can send destruction from either direction? Third: you know what happened to previous nations destroyed by خسف or by stones from the sky, so how can you remain complacent while repeating their تكذيب? Each argument tightens the noose.

On the phrase مَنْ فِي السَّمَاءِ: the point of these ayahs is the threat, not a statement about God's location. The mufassirun converge on this. Zamakhshari explains that السماء is mentioned because it is the seat of God's ملكوت: His angels, throne, preserved tablet, and the source from which His commands descend. Maturidi reads it as إله السماء, the God of the heaven, which does not exclude His being the God of the earth, just as His saying ما يكون من نجوى ثلاثة إلا هو رابعهم does not exclude His presence when two are speaking. The rhetorical function is what matters: the God whose dominion extends to the heavens, not some earthly deity your hands carved, is the one you should fear (Zuhayli). Qurtubi reports that مَنْ may also refer to the ملائكة, since they are the agents of God's commands in the world, and Ibn Ashur accepts this as linguistically viable, since the Quran itself has the angels say إنا مهلكوا أهل هذه القرية (al-Ankabut: 31). All these readings converge on a single force: the warning.

Ibn Kathir frames the restraint behind the threat. God is قادر على تعذيبهم due to their كفر, yet He يحلم ويصفح ويؤجّل ولا يعجّل: He forbears, pardons, delays, and does not hasten. The threat is a mercy because it precedes the act.

## Ayah 18

> وَلَقَدْ كَذَّبَ الَّذِينَ مِنْ قَبْلِهِمْ فَكَيْفَ كَانَ نَكِيرِ

The threat is not hypothetical. It has precedent.

The most striking thing about this ayah is what Ibn Ashur calls the الْتِفات (rhetorical shift). Throughout ayahs 13-17, God addressed the مشركون directly: أسروا, أأمنتم, ستعلمون. Now, suddenly, He speaks about them in the third person: الذين من قبلهم. He did not say ولقد كذب الذين من قبلكم. The shift from "you" to "them" is a sign of غضب (anger) and إبعاد (distancing). They have been stripped of the عزّ الحضور, the honour of being spoken to directly, as though their persistent تكذيب has cost them the right to be addressed. And yet God does not cut them off entirely; they are still listening, still hearing their story told in the third person. The door is not locked, but the speaker has turned away.

The emphatic ولقد serves a specific function. The لام and قد together convey تأكيد (emphatic assertion), and Ibn Ashur explains the تنزيل: the مشركون are treated as though they might believe the previous nations were punished for reasons other than تكذيب. The emphatic construction corrects this: no, it was specifically their denial of the messengers that brought the punishment.

Maturidi reads the ayah as ارتداع (deterrence). God reminds them of what befell previous deniers so that they might desist. He adds a pointed implicit answer to the rhetorical question فكيف كان نكيري: أليس وجدوه شديدا وحقا, "did they not find it severe and true?" The question is not asking; it is asserting. The نكير was devastating. The evidence is in the ruins of عاد, ثمود, قوم لوط, قوم فرعون, and others that the Arabs of Makkah knew firsthand. They traded through their remains and camped among their ruins.

The word نكير is a مصدر بمعنى الإنكار (Zamakhshari, Ibn Ashur): God's act of denial and repudiation through punishment. It is the mirror of their تكذيب. They denied the messengers; God denied them. The correspondence is exact.
