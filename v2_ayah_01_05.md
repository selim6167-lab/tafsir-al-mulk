# Surah Al-Mulk (67): Ayahs 1-5

## The Surah's Identity

The surah carries several names, each capturing a different aspect of its function. Beyond the standard **الملك**, the Prophet (peace be upon him) called it **المانعة** (the Protector): Ibn Mas'ud reports, "We used to call it al-Mani'ah in the Prophet's time" (Tabrani, via Ibn Ashur). He also called it **المنجية** (the Saviour) because "it saves from the punishment of the grave" (Tirmidhi). Perhaps most telling is the name **المجادلة** (the Advocate), from Ibn Abbas, because it argues on behalf of its reciter in the grave (Ibn Ashur via al-Razi). A surah named after sovereignty (الملك) that functions as personal protection and advocacy: this tension between cosmic scale and intimate care runs through all thirty ayahs.

The surah is Makki by consensus (Qurtubi: "في قول الجميع"). The Prophet said: "A surah of thirty ayahs interceded for a man until he was forgiven: تبارك الذي بيده الملك" (Tirmidhi, graded حسن). And: "I wish that تبارك were in the heart of every believer" (al-Tha'labi, via Qurtubi).

## Ayah 1

> تَبَارَكَ الَّذِي بِيَدِهِ الْمُلْكُ وَهُوَ عَلَى كُلِّ شَيْءٍ قَدِيرٌ

The surah opens not with a command, a story, or an oath, but with God glorifying Himself. The verb تبارك is exclusive to God in the Quran; it is never used for any creature (Zuhayli). It comes from the root ب-ر-ك (بركة), and its core meaning is وفرة الخير, the abundance and overflow of good. Ibn Ashur gives the clearest reading: تبارك indicates المبالغة في وفرة الخير, that every conceivable form of perfection is intrinsically, inexhaustibly present in God. The صيغة التفاعل, when applied to a single subject, conveys intensity and fullness (Ibn Ashur). Zuhayli echoes this: تعالى وتعاظم بالذات عن كل ما سواه, وكثير خيره وإنعامه. He glosses البركة as النماء والزيادة الحسية أو المعنوية: growth and increase, whether tangible or intangible. God is the source of ever-increasing, inexhaustible خير, and تبارك is a word that can only be said of Him.

Others glossed it as تقدّس (al-Hasan al-Basri, via Qurtubi) or as دام, the Eternal with no beginning and no end (Qurtubi). Maturidi offers a different angle: he reads بركة as a كناية for the negation of every flaw, comparing it to the Quran's description of rain as ماء مباركا (water free of impurity), meaning God is exalted beyond having any likeness, partner, or deficiency. This reading, however, drifts into the semantic territory of سبحان (تنزيه, the negation of imperfection), which is a different concept from بركة (the overflowing of intrinsic good). The root ب-ر-ك is about fullness and abundance, not about the negation of flaws. Maturidi was a Central Asian scholar from Samarkand, not a native Arabic speaker, and this may account for a slight drift in root-level intuition. His gloss is worth noting, but the stronger reading stays with the root: God is not merely free from defect (that would be سبحان), He is the overflowing source of all good.

The expression بيده الملك is a تمثيل (representative image): complete control likened to someone holding something in his hand (Zamakhshari). What matters is the force of the image. Ibn Abbas unpacks it concretely: "He dignifies whom He wills and humbles whom He wills, gives life and causes death, enriches and impoverishes, gives and withholds" (Qurtubi). Ibn Ashur adds a precision that matters: الملك covers both مُلك (sovereign authority, the power to command and enforce) and مِلك (ownership, the exclusive right of disposal). God holds both perfectly, whereas any human ruler holds, at best, one partially.

The closing وهو على كل شيء قدير is not mere emphasis. The word قدير is صيغة المبالغة (intensive form): His power is not just present but inexhaustible, covering both what exists and what could exist (Zamakhshari).

**On the word الذي**: the use of the relative pronoun rather than a divine name achieves تفخيم وتعظيم (magnification). It forces the listener to wait, to ask "who?", and then be answered through the description itself (Zuhayli). The entire ayah is the answer to an implied question.

## Ayah 2

> الَّذِي خَلَقَ الْمَوْتَ وَالْحَيَاةَ لِيَبْلُوَكُمْ أَيُّكُمْ أَحْسَنُ عَمَلًا وَهُوَ الْعَزِيزُ الْغَفُورُ

### Death before life

The first thing this ayah does is something unexpected: it places death before life. The strongest explanation is practical and psychological: death is the more powerful motivator. Zamakhshari states it directly: أقوى الناس داعيا إلى العمل من نصب موته بين عينيه, the person most powerfully driven to act is the one who keeps his death before his eyes. Death was placed first because, for the purpose of this ayah (motivating the best عمل), it is more important. Qurtubi and Baghawi add that death carries greater قهر (subjugation), fitting the context of divine majesty that opened the surah. Ibn Ashur explains: since the purpose is ليبلوكم أيكم أحسن عملا, and since death is the mechanism that closes the test and delivers you to the reckoning, mentioning it first foregrounds the stakes.

A weaker interpretation holds that death is temporally prior, that things begin in a state of non-existence, as the نطفة is lifeless before it is ensouled (Qurtubi). But this conflates two different Arabic concepts. الموت in the Quran is an event, not a state. The Quran consistently treats it as something that "comes" (يأتي), is "tasted" (ذائقة الموت), and happens "after" a prior condition (بعد). These are all event markers. The state of being dead or non-living uses the adjective مَيِّت or أَمْوَات, not the masdar الموت. When 2:28 describes the pre-birth condition, it says كنتم أَمْوَاتًا (the adjective, a state), not الموت. And 44:56 confirms death is a one-time event: لا يذوقون فيها الموت إلا الموتة الأولى. Once the event happens, it is done. So the claim "death is mentioned first because we were in a state of non-existence" confuses the state (أموات) with the event (الموت). The ayah is about the created event of death, not the condition of non-being.

The verb خلق applied to death tells you something important: death is not merely the absence of life but a created reality in its own right (Ibn Kathir, Zuhayli). Evidence from hadith: on the Day of Judgement, death will be brought in the form of a كبش أملح (a black-and-white ram) and slaughtered between Paradise and Hell. A caller will announce: "O people of Paradise, eternity with no death! O people of the Fire, eternity with no death!" (Ibn Kathir). Death has form; it is something made. And once the event is destroyed, no one will ever experience it again. This confirms death as a discrete, created event, not a mere absence.

### The test (ليبلوكم) as the surah's architectural lens

The purpose clause ليبلوكم أيكم أحسن عملا is not about God needing to find out. All tafasir agree: the ابتلاء (test) is for the benefit of the tested, to make manifest to them their own reality (Maturidi, Zamakhshari, Qurtubi).

Ibn Ashur makes a point that deserves to be treated as the interpretive key to the entire surah. He observes that تعليل فعل بعلة لا يقتضي انحصار علله في العلة المذكورة: stating one reason for an act does not mean there are no other reasons. The Quran chose ليبلوكم for this مقام, this particular context. That choice is the surah's thesis statement, because the entire structure of Al-Mulk is an ابتلاء architecture:

1. **The Examiner's credentials** (ayah 1): the One who administers the test is introduced, His sovereignty and power established.
2. **The test announced** (ayah 2): ليبلوكم أيكم أحسن عملا.
3. **Evidence presented for examination** (ayahs 3-5): the sky's perfection, the challenge to find a flaw, the stars. The student is shown the exam material.
4. **Consequences of failure** (ayahs 6-11): the inmates of Hell confess, لو كنا نسمع أو نعقل ما كنا في أصحاب السعير.
5. **Consequences of passing** (ayah 12): إن الذين يخشون ربهم بالغيب لهم مغفرة وأجر كبير.
6. **The Examiner's omniscience** (ayahs 13-14): He knows what is concealed and what is declared. The proctor sees everything.
7. **The test environment** (ayah 15): the Earth made ذلولا, traversable, a prepared examination hall.
8. **The stakes** (ayahs 16-18): what if He caused the earth to swallow you, or sent a stone-bearing wind?
9. **Test questions** (ayahs 19-21): do you not see the birds? Who is it that provides for you? The questions are posed.
10. **The tools given match the confession of the failed**: ayah 23 lists what God gave the test-takers (السمع والأبصار والأفئدة), and this directly echoes the confession at ayah 10 (لو كنا نسمع أو نعقل). The failed had the tools. They did not use them.
11. **Collection for results** (ayah 24): He is the one who dispersed you on earth and to Him you will be gathered.
12. **The surah ends with an unanswered question** (ayah 30): أرأيتم إن أصبح ماؤكم غورا فمن يأتيكم بماء معين. No answer is given, because the test is still running. The question hangs in the air for every listener to answer with their life.

The word ابتلاء is not incidental; it is the surah's spine.

### What أحسن عملا means

But what does أحسن عملا actually mean? The Prophet himself explained it: "which of you has the best understanding, the most restraint from God's prohibitions, and the swiftest obedience" (Baghawi, Qurtubi). Al-Fudayl ibn Iyad defined it more tightly: أخلصه وأصوبه, the most sincere and the most correct. Sincere means for God's sake alone; correct means according to the Sunnah. Neither quality alone is enough (Zamakhshari, Baghawi). Sincerity without correctness is misguided devotion; correctness without sincerity is performance.

### Death and life as twin instruments

Maturidi reads the ayah through a different lens. Death is an object of رهبة (dread) and life an object of رغبة (desire). The test operates through both: are you deterred by the dread of death from evil? Are you drawn by the love of life toward the eternal life, not the fleeting one? Both death and life are tools of the test, not just its backdrop.

He then draws a remarkable chain: the creation of death and life proves the necessity of البعث (resurrection), because the aspirant can only reach what he aspires to through resurrection. And resurrection proves the necessity of الرسالة (prophethood), because the realities of reward and punishment are unseen and require a messenger to convey them. One ayah, and you arrive at the entire structure of Islamic theology.

### The closing names

The closing العزيز الغفور is not decorative. Ibn Ashur identifies it as a تذييل (capping statement) whose two names map precisely onto the two possible outcomes of the test: العزيز addresses the disobedient (warning of retribution from the Mighty), while الغفور addresses the obedient (promise of pardon from the Forgiving). Maturidi adds: these names clarify that God's testing is not for His own benefit, since He is العزيز (self-sufficient in His might). The test exists entirely for the tested.

## Ayahs 3-4

> الَّذِي خَلَقَ سَبْعَ سَمَاوَاتٍ طِبَاقًا مَا تَرَى فِي خَلْقِ الرَّحْمَنِ مِنْ تَفَاوُتٍ فَارْجِعِ الْبَصَرَ هَلْ تَرَى مِنْ فُطُورٍ
>
> ثُمَّ ارْجِعِ الْبَصَرَ كَرَّتَيْنِ يَنْقَلِبْ إِلَيْكَ الْبَصَرُ خَاسِئًا وَهُوَ حَسِيرٌ

The surah moves from the invisible (death) to the most visible thing in creation: the sky. Seven heavens, طباقا, layered one above the other (Ibn Kathir, Qurtubi, Baghawi). Ibn Ashur suggests طباقا may also mean متماثلة (matching), signifying not just vertical stacking but systematic correspondence in properties like sphericity and orbital motion.

### Two types of imperfection, and neither exists

Then comes the central claim, and it unfolds as an escalating challenge. The Quran asks the observer to search for two distinct kinds of defect, and the Arabic is precise about what each one means.

**تفاوت** (root ف-و-ت, core meaning: الفوت, the gap or distance between two things, what slips past or misses its counterpart). This is systemic design incoherence. The parts of a system that should correspond do not match; components that should work in concert miss each other, like gears that do not mesh. Zamakhshari gives the linguistic core: عدم التناسب, كأن بعض الشيء يفوت بعضا ولا يلائمه, "a lack of proportionality, as though one part of the thing slips past another and does not fit it." If تفاوت existed in the sky, it would look like this: gravity behaving differently in different regions; orbits that do not cohere; a system whose parts are individually functional but collectively incoherent, as if designed by committee.

**فطور** (root ف-ط-ر, core meaning: الشّقُّ طولاً, a lengthwise crack, the kind of split that runs through material that was meant to be whole; the Arabs say سيف فُطار, a cracked sword). This is structural or material failure. Not a design problem but a construction problem: the thing is cracking, coming apart, failing under the strain of its own existence. If فطور existed in the sky, it would look like this: visible seams in the firmament, fractures where the fabric of the cosmos gives way, a ceiling showing structural stress.

The challenge escalates through three stages:

1. **Find design flaws** (تفاوت): look at the system as a whole. Do the parts cohere? مَا تَرَى فِي خَلْقِ الرَّحْمَنِ مِنْ تَفَاوُتٍ. The answer is no.
2. **Find structural cracks** (فطور): forget the system. Just look at the material. فَارْجِعِ الْبَصَرَ هَلْ تَرَى مِنْ فُطُورٍ. The answer is still no.
3. **Keep looking** (كرتين): ثُمَّ ارْجِعِ الْبَصَرَ كَرَّتَيْنِ. The word كرتين is formally a dual (two) but every major mufassir reads it as كناية عن التكثير, a figure for multiplicity (Zamakhshari, Qurtubi, Ibn Ashur). The analogy is to لبيك وسعديك, which does not mean "I respond to you twice" but "I respond to you endlessly" (Zamakhshari). No matter how many times you look, the result is the same.

And what does the gaze find? Your gaze returns transformed. The Quran does not say يرجع (simple return); it says يَنْقَلِبْ. The root ق-ل-ب means to flip, overturn, transform. يرجع would mean the gaze goes out and comes back, the same gaze, in the same state. ينقلب means it comes back changed. It went out confident, searching, intent on finding a flaw. It returns overturned by the experience. The sky did something to it. The verb encodes the transformation, not just the return.

Two words describe this transformed gaze, each devastating in its own way. خاسئا: from خسأ, to drive away as one drives away a dog (Qurtubi). The gaze is not merely unsuccessful; it is repelled, rebuffed, sent back with صغار (humiliation) (Zamakhshari). حسير: worn out, exhausted from the effort of searching (Ibn Abbas, Mujahid, Qatadah, via Ibn Kathir). Zamakhshari captures the combined force: the twin qualities of خاسئ and حسير represent repulsion (you found nothing) and fatigue (you exhausted yourself trying). The sky defeats the investigator.

### Why الرحمن and not الله

Zamakhshari makes a crucial observation about the phrasing. The natural expression would have been ما ترى فيهنّ (in them), but the Quran substitutes في خلق الرحمن. This achieves two things: it magnifies the act of creating them (تعظيم خلقهن), and it provides the reason for their perfection. They are the creation of الرحمن, whose power produces only what is flawless.

Ibn Ashur extends this further: the choice of the name الرحمن (rather than الله or a pronoun) is an إيماء (allusion) that the perfection of creation is itself an expression of God's رحمة (mercy). If there were تفاوت in creation, the نظام (system) upon which human welfare depends would collapse. Cosmic perfection is not aesthetic luxury; it is mercy.

### The absence of defect as proof of توحيد

Maturidi takes the argument in a direction no other mufassir does. If one observed فطور (cracks) in creation, one would suspect multiple creators (تمانع), because conflict and fracture arise from plurality: ما يبني هذا يهدمه الآخر (what one builds, the other demolishes). The absence of فطور is a direct proof of توحيد. And if one observed تفاوت (design incoherence), one would suspect سفه (folly) in the designer, a negation of حكمة. The absence of both, taken together, proves وحدانية (oneness) and حكمة (wisdom) simultaneously.

**On the two قراءات**: The majority read تَفَاوُت (tafawut). Hamzah and al-Kisa'i read تَفَوُّت (tafawwut). Both are valid لغتان, like تعاهد/تعهد (Qurtubi). The root meaning is الفوت (gap, distance); the تفاعل form here conveys intensity, not reciprocity (Ibn Ashur).

Qurtubi cites a line of poetry that captures the combined force of ayahs 3-4:

> مَنْ مَدَّ طَرْفًا إِلَى مَا فَوْقَ غَايَتِهِ / ارْتَدَّ خَسْآنَ مِنْهُ الطَّرْفُ قَدْ حَسِرَا

"Whoever stretches his gaze beyond his limit, his gaze returns to him repelled and worn."

## Ayah 5

> وَلَقَدْ زَيَّنَّا السَّمَاءَ الدُّنْيَا بِمَصَابِيحَ وَجَعَلْنَاهَا رُجُومًا لِلشَّيَاطِينِ وَأَعْتَدْنَا لَهُمْ عَذَابَ السَّعِيرِ

After proving the sky has no defects (ayah 3) and challenging anyone to find one (ayah 4), God now reveals what He put in the sky: beauty and purpose. Ibn Ashur notes that the emphatic لقد connects to the preceding challenge: "Do you see any فطور? Indeed, We have adorned it." The answer to the challenge is not just absence of defect but presence of splendor.

The stars are called مصابيح (lamps), a تشبيه بليغ (compressed simile) comparing them to lanterns for their luminosity (Ibn Ashur). Zamakhshari adds a visceral image: the sky is "the roof of the house in which you gather" (سقف الدار التي اجتمعتم فيها), adorned with lamps of incomparable brightness. The indefiniteness of مصابيح conveys تعظيم: "What lamps! Lamps whose brilliance no earthly lamp can match" (Zamakhshari).

The السماء الدنيا means the nearest heaven, the one closest to earth. Maturidi makes a small but useful clarification: الدنيا here is the feminine of الأدنى (nearest), not a reference to worldly life. Its counterpart is القصوى (the farthest), not الآخرة.

Three functions of the stars are identified, based on a narration from Qatadah: (1) زينة للسماء (adornment), (2) رجوما للشياطين (missiles against devils who try to eavesdrop on heavenly councils), and (3) علامات يهتدى بها (signs for navigation). Qatadah then added: "Whoever interprets them beyond these has burdened himself with what he has no knowledge of" (Ibn Kathir, Qurtubi, Baghawi). This warning was almost certainly aimed at astrologers and soothsayers (كهّان) who claimed to read fates in the stars. It should not be read as a general prohibition on studying or understanding celestial bodies. The Quran itself says وسخّر لكم ما في السماوات وما في الأرض جميعا منه (45:13), an open-ended statement of subjugation for human benefit. Qatadah was a respected tabi'i, but his opinion is not binding revelation; it reflects his historical context and the astrologers of his time.

The obvious question: if stars are permanent fixtures, how are they also projectiles? The best answer across the tafasir: the شهب (shooting stars) that strike the devils are not the actual كواكب but emanations from them, like a spark taken from a fire that remains intact (Ibn Kathir, Zamakhshari, Qurtubi). The Quran itself confirms this: إلا من خطف الخطفة فأتبعه شهاب ثاقب (Zamakhshari).

Maturidi offers the most layered reading of this ayah. He identifies three distinct نعم (blessings) in the stars: (1) beauty for the beholders, especially at night when the beauty of the earth is hidden; (2) guidance in darkness at land and sea; (3) protection from the devils' corruption of heavenly news, ensuring revelation reaches humanity pure through the prophets rather than distorted through the كهّان (soothsayers). He then adds a subtle observation: the beauty of the sky at night is a divine compensation. When the زينة of the earth (its colours, forms, landscapes) becomes invisible in darkness, God replaces it with a different, more remarkable beauty in the sky, one visible from any distance. Night does not leave you without splendor; it changes the source.

The closing وأعتدنا لهم عذاب السعير extends the punishment beyond the شهب. Being pelted with shooting stars in this world does not discharge the full penalty; the fire of the hereafter is a separate, permanent retribution that is already prepared and waiting (Maturidi). The past tense أعتدنا (We have prepared) signals that the punishment is not a future possibility but an accomplished fact.
