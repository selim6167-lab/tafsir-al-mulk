# Surah Al-Mulk (67): Ayahs 1-5

## The Surah's Identity

The surah carries several names, each capturing a different aspect of its function. Beyond the standard **الملك**, the Prophet (peace be upon him) called it **المانعة** (the Protector): Ibn Mas'ud reports, "We used to call it al-Mani'ah in the Prophet's time" (Tabrani, via Ibn Ashur). He also called it **المنجية** (the Saviour) because "it saves from the punishment of the grave" (Tirmidhi). Perhaps most telling is the name **المجادلة** (the Advocate), from Ibn Abbas, because it argues on behalf of its reciter in the grave (Ibn Ashur via al-Razi). A surah named after sovereignty (الملك) that functions as personal protection and advocacy: this tension between cosmic scale and intimate care runs through all thirty ayahs.

The surah is Makki by consensus (Qurtubi: "في قول الجميع"). The Prophet said: "A surah of thirty ayahs interceded for a man until he was forgiven: تبارك الذي بيده الملك" (Tirmidhi, graded حسن). And: "I wish that تبارك were in the heart of every believer" (al-Tha'labi, via Qurtubi).

## Ayah 1

> تَبَارَكَ الَّذِي بِيَدِهِ الْمُلْكُ وَهُوَ عَلَى كُلِّ شَيْءٍ قَدِيرٌ

The surah opens not with a command, a story, or an oath, but with God glorifying Himself. The verb تبارك is exclusive to God in the Quran; it is never used for any creature (Zuhayli). It comes from the root ب-ر-ك (بركة), and Maturidi offers the sharpest reading of its meaning here: بركة is a kinayah for the negation of every flaw. Just as the Quran describes rain as ماء مباركا (water free of impurity), تبارك means God is exalted beyond having any likeness, partner, or deficiency. Others glossed it as تقدّس (al-Hasan al-Basri, via Qurtubi) or as دام, the Eternal with no beginning and no end (Qurtubi).

The expression بيده الملك is a تمثيل (representative image): complete control likened to someone holding something in his hand (Zamakhshari). What matters is the force of the image. Ibn Abbas unpacks it concretely: "He dignifies whom He wills and humbles whom He wills, gives life and causes death, enriches and impoverishes, gives and withholds" (Qurtubi). Ibn Ashur adds a precision that matters: الملك covers both مُلك (sovereign authority, the power to command and enforce) and مِلك (ownership, the exclusive right of disposal). God holds both perfectly, whereas any human ruler holds, at best, one partially.

The closing وهو على كل شيء قدير is not mere emphasis. The word قدير is صيغة المبالغة (intensive form): His power is not just present but inexhaustible, covering both what exists and what could exist (Zamakhshari).

**On the word الذي**: the use of the relative pronoun rather than a divine name achieves تفخيم وتعظيم (magnification). It forces the listener to wait, to ask "who?", and then be answered through the description itself (Zuhayli). The entire ayah is the answer to an implied question.

## Ayah 2

> الَّذِي خَلَقَ الْمَوْتَ وَالْحَيَاةَ لِيَبْلُوَكُمْ أَيُّكُمْ أَحْسَنُ عَمَلًا وَهُوَ الْعَزِيزُ الْغَفُورُ

The first thing this ayah does is something unexpected: it places death before life. Three explanations stand out. First, death carries a greater force of قهر (subjugation), fitting the context of divine majesty that opened the surah (Qurtubi, Baghawi). Second, death is temporally prior: things begin in a state of non-existence, as the نطفة is lifeless before it is ensouled (Qurtubi). Third, and most practically: death is the stronger motivator. Contemplating mortality is the most powerful spur to action (Zamakhshari, Zuhayli). The surah's opening move is to confront you with your end before your beginning.

The verb خلق applied to death tells you something important: death is not merely the absence of life but a created reality in its own right (Ibn Kathir, Zuhayli). Evidence from hadith: on the Day of Judgement, death will be brought in the form of a كبش أملح (a black-and-white ram) and slaughtered between Paradise and Hell (Ibn Kathir). Death has form; it is something made.

The purpose clause ليبلوكم أيكم أحسن عملا is not about God needing to find out. All tafasir agree: the ابتلاء (test) is for the benefit of the tested, to make manifest to them their own reality (Maturidi, Zamakhshari, Qurtubi). But what does أحسن عملا actually mean? The Prophet himself explained it: "which of you has the best understanding, the most restraint from God's prohibitions, and the swiftest obedience" (Baghawi, Qurtubi). Al-Fudayl ibn Iyad defined it more tightly: أخلصه وأصوبه, the most sincere and the most correct. Sincere means for God's sake alone; correct means according to the Sunnah. Neither quality alone is enough (Zamakhshari, Baghawi). Sincerity without correctness is misguided devotion; correctness without sincerity is performance.

Maturidi reads the ayah through a different lens entirely. Death is an object of رهبة (dread) and life an object of رغبة (desire). The test operates through both: are you deterred by the dread of death from evil? Are you drawn by the love of life toward the eternal life, not the fleeting one? Both death and life are tools of the test, not just its backdrop.

He then draws a remarkable chain: the creation of death and life proves the necessity of البعث (resurrection), because the aspirant can only reach what he aspires to through resurrection. And resurrection proves the necessity of الرسالة (prophethood), because the realities of reward and punishment are unseen and require a messenger to convey them. One ayah, and you arrive at the entire structure of Islamic theology.

The closing العزيز الغفور is not decorative. Ibn Ashur identifies it as a تذييل (capping statement) whose two names map precisely onto the two possible outcomes of the test: العزيز addresses the disobedient (warning of retribution from the Mighty), while الغفور addresses the obedient (promise of pardon from the Forgiving). Maturidi adds: these names clarify that God's testing is not for His own benefit, since He is العزيز (self-sufficient in His might). The test exists entirely for the tested.

## Ayah 3

> الَّذِي خَلَقَ سَبْعَ سَمَاوَاتٍ طِبَاقًا مَا تَرَى فِي خَلْقِ الرَّحْمَنِ مِنْ تَفَاوُتٍ فَارْجِعِ الْبَصَرَ هَلْ تَرَى مِنْ فُطُورٍ

The surah moves from the invisible (death) to the most visible thing in creation: the sky. Seven heavens, طباقا, layered one above the other (Ibn Kathir, Qurtubi, Baghawi). Ibn Ashur suggests طباقا may also mean متماثلة (matching), signifying not just vertical stacking but systematic correspondence in properties like sphericity and orbital motion.

Then comes the central claim: ما ترى في خلق الرحمن من تفاوت. No inconsistency, no discordance, no defect. Zamakhshari makes a crucial observation about the phrasing. The natural expression would have been ما ترى فيهنّ (in them), but the Quran substitutes في خلق الرحمن. This achieves two things: it magnifies the act of creating them (تعظيم خلقهن), and it provides the reason for their perfection. They are the creation of الرحمن, whose power produces only what is flawless.

Ibn Ashur extends this further: the choice of the name الرحمن (rather than الله or a pronoun) is an إيماء (allusion) that the perfection of creation is itself an expression of God's رحمة (mercy). If there were تفاوت in creation, the نظام (system) upon which human welfare depends would collapse. Cosmic perfection is not aesthetic luxury; it is mercy.

Maturidi takes the argument in a direction no other mufassir does. There is no تفاوت in the دلالة (indicative function) of created things: all of them, despite differing in their physical forms, point uniformly to God's وحدانية and حكمة. The heavens differ from the earth, life differs from death, but the coherence of purpose is unbroken. And if one did observe فطور (cracks) in creation, one would suspect multiple creators (تمانع), because conflict and fracture arise from plurality. The absence of فطور is a direct proof of توحيد.

The command فارجع البصر هل ترى من فطور is a challenge. The فاء is تسبيبية (causative): since there is no تفاوت, go verify it yourself (Zamakhshari, Ibn Ashur). Look for شقوق (cracks), صدوع (fractures), خلل (gaps). You will find none (Ibn Kathir, Qurtubi).

**On the two قراءات**: The majority read تَفَاوُت (tafāwut). Hamzah and al-Kisa'i read تَفَوُّت (tafawwut). Both are valid لغتان, like تعاهد/تعهد (Qurtubi). The root meaning is الفوت (gap, distance); the تفاعل form here conveys intensity, not reciprocity (Ibn Ashur).

## Ayah 4

> ثُمَّ ارْجِعِ الْبَصَرَ كَرَّتَيْنِ يَنْقَلِبْ إِلَيْكَ الْبَصَرُ خَاسِئًا وَهُوَ حَسِيرٌ

The first look was not enough. Now God says: do it again, and again, and again. The word كرتين is formally a dual (two) but every major mufassir reads it as كناية عن التكثير, a figure for multiplicity (Zamakhshari, Qurtubi, Ibn Ashur, Zuhayli). The analogy is to لبيك وسعديك, which does not mean "I respond to you twice" but "I respond to you endlessly" (Zamakhshari). The point: no matter how many times you look, the result is the same.

And what is the result? Two words, each devastating in its own way. خاسئا: from خسأ, to drive away as one drives away a dog (Qurtubi). The gaze is not merely unsuccessful; it is repelled, rebuffed, sent back with صغار (humiliation) (Zamakhshari). حسير: worn out, exhausted from the effort of searching (Ibn Abbas, Mujahid, Qatadah, via Ibn Kathir). Zamakhshari captures the combined force: the twin qualities of خاسئ and حسير represent repulsion (you found nothing) and fatigue (you exhausted yourself trying). The sky defeats the investigator.

Ibn Ashur notices something about word choice that others miss. The Quran uses ينقلب here instead of the more obvious يرجع, because رجع has already been used twice in the passage (ارجع... ارجع). And كرتين was chosen over مرتين because كرّة resists the literal dual reading more naturally. Both choices, he argues, are features of إعجاز (inimitability): the Quran avoids even incidental repetition while maintaining precision.

Qurtubi cites a line of poetry that captures the ayah perfectly:

> مَنْ مَدَّ طَرْفًا إِلَى مَا فَوْقَ غَايَتِهِ / ارْتَدَّ خَسْآنَ مِنْهُ الطَّرْفُ قَدْ حَسِرَا

"Whoever stretches his gaze beyond his limit, his gaze returns to him repelled and worn."

## Ayah 5

> وَلَقَدْ زَيَّنَّا السَّمَاءَ الدُّنْيَا بِمَصَابِيحَ وَجَعَلْنَاهَا رُجُومًا لِلشَّيَاطِينِ وَأَعْتَدْنَا لَهُمْ عَذَابَ السَّعِيرِ

After proving the sky has no defects (ayah 3) and challenging anyone to find one (ayah 4), God now reveals what He put in the sky: beauty and purpose. Ibn Ashur notes that the emphatic لقد connects to the preceding challenge: "Do you see any فطور? Indeed, We have adorned it." The answer to the challenge is not just absence of defect but presence of splendor.

The stars are called مصابيح (lamps), a تشبيه بليغ (compressed simile) comparing them to lanterns for their luminosity (Ibn Ashur). Zamakhshari adds a visceral image: the sky is "the roof of the house in which you gather" (سقف الدار التي اجتمعتم فيها), adorned with lamps of incomparable brightness. The indefiniteness of مصابيح conveys تعظيم: "What lamps! Lamps whose brilliance no earthly lamp can match" (Zamakhshari).

The السماء الدنيا means the nearest heaven, the one closest to earth. Maturidi makes a small but useful clarification: الدنيا here is the feminine of الأدنى (nearest), not a reference to worldly life. Its counterpart is القصوى (the farthest), not الآخرة.

Three functions of the stars are identified, based on a narration from Qatadah: (1) زينة للسماء (adornment), (2) رجوما للشياطين (missiles against devils who try to eavesdrop on heavenly councils), and (3) علامات يهتدى بها (signs for navigation). Qatadah then added a warning: "Whoever interprets them beyond these has burdened himself with what he has no knowledge of" (Ibn Kathir, Qurtubi, Baghawi). This is a direct rebuke of astrology.

The obvious question: if stars are permanent fixtures, how are they also projectiles? The best answer across the tafasir: the شهب (shooting stars) that strike the devils are not the actual كواكب but emanations from them, like a spark taken from a fire that remains intact (Ibn Kathir, Zamakhshari, Qurtubi). The Quran itself confirms this: إلا من خطف الخطفة فأتبعه شهاب ثاقب (Zamakhshari).

Maturidi offers the most layered reading of this ayah. He identifies three distinct نعم (blessings) in the stars: (1) beauty for the beholders, especially at night when the beauty of the earth is hidden; (2) guidance in darkness at land and sea; (3) protection from the devils' corruption of heavenly news, ensuring revelation reaches humanity pure through the prophets rather than distorted through the كهّان (soothsayers). He then adds a subtle observation: the beauty of the sky at night is a divine compensation. When the زينة of the earth (its colours, forms, landscapes) becomes invisible in darkness, God replaces it with a different, more remarkable beauty in the sky, one visible from any distance. Night does not leave you without splendor; it changes the source.

The closing وأعتدنا لهم عذاب السعير extends the punishment beyond the شهب. Being pelted with shooting stars in this world does not discharge the full penalty; the fire of the hereafter is a separate, permanent retribution that is already prepared and waiting (Maturidi). The past tense أعتدنا (We have prepared) signals that the punishment is not a future possibility but an accomplished fact.
